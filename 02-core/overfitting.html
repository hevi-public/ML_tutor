Overfitting: memorizing instead of learning

The central occupational disease of machine learning. This page hands you the dial that causes it, so you can feel exactly where learning tips into memorizing.

Before you start Training vs. testing — this page leans entirely on the train/test split.

The idea

Give a model more flexibility and it fits the training data better. Always. More bend, better practice grade — nothing stops that trend, ever. But somewhere along the way, the extra bend stops capturing the pattern and starts wrapping around the noise: the accidents of which particular dots landed where. From that point on, the test grade turns around and gets worse while the training grade keeps improving.

That's overfitting. Its mirror image — a model too stiff to catch the real pattern at all, bad grades everywhere — is underfitting. Between them sits a sweet spot, and finding it is much of the day-to-day craft of ML.

Watch the train grade only ever improve as you add bend — while the test grade improves, bottoms out around 3–5, then climbs. That U-shape is the most important curve in applied ML. At degree 12, look at the curve's wild swings between dots: those lurches ARE memorized noise.

What to do about it

Overfitting has standard treatments, all with the same flavor — make memorizing harder than learning:

  • More data. The cure that always works when you can afford it: noise averages out, patterns don't. Twice the dots makes the bendy curve's tricks visibly fail on its own training set.
  • A simpler model. Turn the dial down. Fewer knobs, less capacity to memorize.
  • Regularization. Keep the flexible model, but charge a fee for wildness: add a penalty to the loss for large, dramatic settings. The model then bends only where the data genuinely pays for it. (The λ from the Greek table is traditionally the fee's size.)
  • Stop early. During long training runs, the model tends to learn the pattern first and memorize the noise later — so watch the validation grade and stop when it turns.
The same idea, one level deeper: bias and variance

Textbooks describe the U-shape as a tug-of-war between two failure styles. A too-stiff model is wrong in a consistent way — re-deal the data and it draws nearly the same too-simple curve every time (high bias: stubborn). A too-bendy model is wrong in an erratic way — re-deal and it draws a wildly different curve each time, because it's fitting each deal's noise (high variance: jumpy).

You can see this yourself: set the dial to 1 and press "re-deal" repeatedly — the line barely moves. Now set it to 12 and re-deal — the curve flails. The sweet spot is where stubbornness and jumpiness are both tolerable. (Yes, "bias" here is a different concept from the bias parameter b — the word is overworked; the glossary entry disambiguates.)
