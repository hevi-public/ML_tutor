Judging classifiers: four ways to be right or wrong

"95% accurate" can describe a great model or a useless one. This page gives you the two questions that expose the difference.

Before you start Probability — models score with numbers like 0.93, and someone must choose where to draw the line. On this page, that someone is you.

The idea

A spam filter's verdict on one email lands in exactly one of four boxes:

  • Spam, flagged as spam — caught it. (A true positive.)
  • Real email, left alone — correct pass. (A true negative.)
  • Real email, flagged as spam — a false alarm: your friend's message dies in the junk folder.
  • Spam, left alone — a miss: junk in your inbox.

Here's the part every practitioner has tattooed somewhere: the two mistakes cost different amounts, and which one costs more depends entirely on the problem. Junk in the inbox is annoying; a real contract offer silently junked is a disaster. For a cancer screen, the costs reverse-and-multiply. No single number can weigh both mistakes for you — which is why "accuracy" alone is never enough.

The two questions, in plain words

  • Precision — "when you point, are you right?" Of everything flagged, what share truly was spam? Low precision = the model cries wolf.
  • Recall — "of what's out there, how much did you find?" Of all actual spam, what share got caught? Low recall = wolves stroll past.

And the trap to retire right now: with rare events, accuracy is nearly meaningless. If 1 email in 1,000 is fraud, the model that says "never fraud" is 99.9% accurate and 100% useless — zero recall; it finds nothing. Whenever you hear an impressive accuracy, your first question is now: "how rare is the thing, and what's the recall?"
