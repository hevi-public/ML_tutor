Vectors & matrices: lists and tables of numbers

The two container types all of ML is built from — and the one operation between them that you'll see a thousand times.

Before you start How to read math helps but isn't required.

The idea

A vector is just a list of numbers — that's the whole definition. What makes it useful is a second way of seeing the same list: as an arrow. The list [3, 1] is also "the arrow you get by walking 3 steps east and 1 step north." One object, two views: a row of boxes when you're storing data, an arrow when you're thinking about direction and similarity.

Why ML cares: every data point is a vector. A house is [size, bedrooms, age]. An email becomes a (very long) list of numbers. The moment data becomes vectors, geometry starts answering questions — "which emails are near this one?" becomes a distance between arrows.

A matrix is the next container up: a table of numbers, or equally, a stack of vectors — one row per data point. A spreadsheet of 1,000 houses × 3 facts is a 1000×3 matrix. That's all "matrix" means.

The one operation to actually learn: the dot product

Take two lists of the same length. Multiply them position by position, add the results up: one number out. That's the dot product.

It has a beautiful secret: that number measures agreement between directions. Arrows pointing the same way → big positive number. Opposite ways → negative. At right angles → zero, "nothing to do with each other."

Try it: drag the arrows

Drag the tips of the two arrows and watch the lists and the dot product react. Try to make the dot product exactly zero — notice what the arrows look like when you succeed.

The dot product is how a model asks "how much does this input look like the pattern I'm tuned for?" — you'll see it inside every neural network layer.

The math (open as much as you like)

The idea in words

Multiply matching positions, add it all up. For [3, 1] · [1, 2.5]: 3×1 + 1×2.5 = 5.5. Positive — and indeed the arrows lean the same way.

Show the math
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i
In plain English The dot product of two lists is: the sum, over every position i, of (a's number there) times (b's number there).

    Bold letters (𝐚) are how math marks "this is a whole list, not one number." In writing you'll also see arrows over letters, or plain letters when context makes it clear.

    One more connection worth planting now: our linear regression prediction ŷ = wx + b generalizes to many inputs as ŷ = 𝐰 · 𝐱 + b — the dot product of a weight list with an input list. "Multiply each input by how much it matters and add up" — the same recipe, any number of inputs.
