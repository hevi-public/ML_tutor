Slopes & gradients: the nudge question

All of calculus that ML needs fits in one question: "if I nudge this a tiny bit, what happens to that?"

Before you start Just How to read math. No calculus memories required — we're rebuilding the one piece ML uses, not the whole course.

The idea

Stand on a hillside. Without opening your eyes you can answer two questions: which way is downhill, and how steep is it. That local, eyes-closed knowledge — steepness right where I'm standing — is what a derivative is.

On a chart: pick a point on a curve and zoom way in. Every curve looks straight up close. The steepness of that little straight piece is the derivative at that point. Positive: the curve is rising there. Negative: falling. Zero: flat — you're standing in a valley bottom or on a hilltop.

Try it: feel the slope

Drag the ball along the hill and watch the dashed line — that's the curve's steepness at the ball, updated live. Then find a spot, press Release the ball, and watch it roll downhill and settle in a valley.

Try releasing the ball from the far left, then from the middle bump. Notice it doesn't always find the deepest valley — just the nearest one downhill. Remember that; it becomes a real issue when training big models.

You just watched gradient descent

The rolling ball is not a metaphor we bolted on — it's literally the algorithm. In the linear regression demo, "let the computer fit it" does exactly what the ball does: check the slope where you stand, step downhill, repeat. The hill there is the loss (the model's grade) and the ball's position is the model's settings.

One upgrade and you have the real thing: real models have many settings, not one. The hill becomes a landscape with many directions, and the slope becomes a list of slopes — one per setting: "nudging w does this, nudging b does that." A list of numbers is a vector (last page!), and this particular vector has a name: the gradient. It points uphill; training steps the other way.

The math (open as much as you like)

The idea in words

The derivative of f at a point is: nudge the input a tiny bit, see how much the output moves, divide "output change" by "input change." It's a sensitivity: how much bang per unit of nudge, right here.

Show the math
\frac{df}{dx} \approx \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}
In plain English The slope of f at x is roughly: (f a nudge later, minus f now), divided by the size of the nudge — for a very small nudge h.

    When several inputs exist, we write ∂ instead of d and nudge one input at a time. The full gradient just collects them:

    \nabla L = \left[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w},\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial b} \right]
    In plain English The gradient of the loss is the list: (how the loss reacts to nudging w, how it reacts to nudging b). One slope per knob, bundled into a vector.

      This is also literally how the demo works — the code computes (f(x + 0.0001) - f(x - 0.0001)) / 0.0002. Real ML libraries get exact derivatives with cleverer bookkeeping (Unit 8 builds one), but the meaning is identical: nudge, measure, divide.
