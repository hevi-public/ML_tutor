Unit 1 · Math foundations

How to read math

Math notation is not knowledge — it's an alphabet. This page teaches you to sound it out, so a wall of symbols becomes a sentence you can read slowly.

Before you start Nothing. This page exists so that every other page needs less courage.

The secret: equations are sentences

Every equation is a sentence with a verb in the middle. The verb is usually =, and it reads "is". Left of it: the thing being defined. Right of it: the recipe for it. So ŷ = wx + b is just the sentence "the prediction is the input times a weight, plus an offset." Once you spot the verb, you read the rest a piece at a time — never all at once.

The three habits that make math readable:

  • Find the verb first (=, ≈, ←) and split the sentence there.
  • Sound out symbols by name — "theta", "sum of", "y-hat". A symbol you can say stops being scary.
  • Letters are just labeled boxes. There's nothing special about x — it's a box someone labeled tersely. Mentally rename it: x is "the temperature", w is "how much it matters".

Try it: read an equation piece by piece

This is the formula for a plain old average — something you already understand. Step through it and watch familiar knowledge put on fancy clothes:

\mu = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i

Permission slip Reading math slowly is not a remedial skill — it's the skill. People fluent in this stuff read equations one piece at a time, exactly like the stepper above. They're just quicker about it, because every symbol is an old friend.
