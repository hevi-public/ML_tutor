Probability & statistics: the math of "usually"

Models guess. Probability is the honest language for guessing — and statistics is how we summarize a pile of examples into a few useful numbers.

Before you start How to read math — we use μ and σ from the Greek letter table (both re-explained here).

Probability: what 70% actually means

"70% chance of rain" doesn't mean it will 70%-rain. It means: out of many days exactly like this one, about 70 in 100 turn rainy. Probability is long-run bookkeeping — a single event just happens or doesn't; the number describes the pattern across many repeats.

ML speaks this language constantly. A spam filter doesn't say "spam"; it says "0.93" — of emails that look like this, about 93 in 100 turn out to be spam. Someone (you, in Unit 2's metrics page) then chooses where to draw the line.

Statistics: a pile of numbers, summarized in two

Given a thousand measurements, two numbers tell most of the story:

  • The mean (μ) — the plain average. Where the pile is centered.
  • The standard deviation (σ) — the typical distance from that center. Are values huddled tight or scattered wide?

The shape of the pile is called a distribution. One shape appears everywhere — measurement errors, heights, averages of anything: the bell curve, or normal distribution. Its handy rule of thumb: about 68% of values fall within one σ of the mean, about 95% within two σ.

The math (open as much as you like)

The idea in words

Mean: add everything, divide by the count. Standard deviation: measure each value's distance from the mean, average the squared distances (that average is the variance), then un-square at the end to get back to normal units.

Show the math
\mu = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu)^2}
In plain English The mean is: add all the values, divide by how many. The standard deviation is: take each value's gap from the mean, square it, average those, then take the square root.

    Look closely at σ's recipe: average of squared misses — you've met that pattern before. Mean squared error is exactly a "variance of the misses." The math of Unit 1 and the ML of Unit 3 are the same small toolbox.
