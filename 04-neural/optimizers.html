Optimizers: how to spend a gradient

Backprop tells you which way is downhill. It says nothing about how boldly to walk. That decision has fifty years of craft in it — and one race settles the intuition.

Before you start Slopes & gradients and backpropagation.

The idea

Real loss landscapes are rarely nice round bowls. The classic villain is the narrow valley: steep walls across, a long gentle slope along. Plain gradient descent (SGD) suffers there, because the gradient mostly points at the far wall, not down the valley — so it zigzags wall-to-wall while inching along the floor. Two classic upgrades:

  • Momentum: give the ball weight. Keep a running velocity; each gradient pushes it rather than dictating the step. Zigzag pushes alternate and cancel; the steady down-valley push accumulates. A heavy ball rolls through the chatter.
  • Adam: give every knob its own step size. Track each parameter's typical gradient size and divide by it — loud directions get tamed, whisper-quiet ones get amplified. Roughly momentum + per-knob auto-scaling; it works well with little tuning, which is why it's the default in half the papers ever written.

The vocabulary you'll meet in the wild

  • The "stochastic" in SGD: real training computes each gradient on a small random mini-batch of examples, not the whole dataset — far cheaper per step, and the noise it adds is mostly harmless, sometimes even helpful. One full lap through the data is an epoch.
  • Learning rate schedules: start bolder, end finer — decaying the step size over training is standard practice, for exactly the reason your intuition suggests.
  • What actually matters: the learning rate is the single most consequential hyperparameter in deep learning. When training diverges (loss exploding) — rate too high. When it crawls — too low. You've now seen both failure modes with your own eyes, which puts you ahead of the folklore.
