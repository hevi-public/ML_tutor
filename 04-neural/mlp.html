Unit 4 · Neural networks

Stacking neurons: where the bends come from

One neuron draws one bent line. A layer of them, summed, can draw bumps, steps, and wiggles — and with enough neurons, any shape at all.

Before you start The neuron. This page is one idea, built with your own hands.

The idea

Wire neurons in layers: every input feeds every neuron in the first layer (a hidden layer — "hidden" only means "between input and output", nothing spookier), those outputs feed the next layer, and so on to the final answer. This assembly is a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) — the plain bread-and-butter neural network.

The question that matters: where does the expressive power come from? The demo below lets you feel the answer. A single ReLU neuron contributes a humble bent ramp: flat, then a slope, one kink. But sums of ramps make shapes. Two opposing ramps make a plateau. Three make a bump. More make wiggles, staircases, anything — the kinks are little joints, and with enough joints a chain of straight pieces can trace any curve. That, informally, is the famous universal approximation property.

You are doing, by hand, exactly what training does automatically: choosing each neuron's slope, position, and contribution so the total curve takes a wanted shape. Training = a machine turning these same knobs, guided by gradients. Nothing else is new.

From curves to boundaries

The demo is one-dimensional for clarity, but the same story holds in 2-D and beyond: each hidden ReLU neuron contributes a crease along a line, and sums of creases sculpt curved decision boundaries — the bent borders that logistic regression, forever straight, could never draw. You'll watch this happen live in the playground lab after two more short pages.

Two honest footnotes while the idea is fresh:

  • "Any shape" ≠ "easily found." Universal approximation says a wide-enough layer could represent your function — not that gradient descent will find those knob settings, or that you have enough data to justify them. Depth (more layers) often finds structure with far fewer neurons than one absurdly wide layer would need.
  • More neurons = more bendiness = the Unit 2 dial. A huge network on scarce data can carve a private kink around every stray dot. The overfitting story never went away; it just got more knobs.
