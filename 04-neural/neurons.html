Unit 4 · Neural networks

The neuron: a weighted sum with an attitude

Strip the mystique: a neuron is the linear recipe you already know, plus one bend. Everything "neural" is built from this.

Before you start Logistic regression — because, surprise: you've already trained a neuron.

The idea

A neuron does exactly three things:

  1. Multiply each input by a weight (how much it matters).
  2. Add them up, plus a bias (the starting offset).
  3. Pass the total through a simple bending function — the activation function.

Steps 1–2 are literally linear regression. Step 3 is the only new ingredient, and logistic regression already did a version of it (the sigmoid). So: a neuron is logistic regression wearing a biology costume. The brain metaphor ("fires when stimulation crosses a threshold") is where the name came from, but you lose nothing by thinking "weighted sum, then bend."

Why the bend matters so much: if you stack pure weighted sums on top of each other, the stack collapses — a sum of sums is just another sum, so a hundred linear layers can only ever draw the same straight boundary as one. The activation's little nonlinearity is what stops the collapse and lets stacking buy new power. No bend, no deep learning. That's the whole secret, this early in the unit.

The math (open as much as you like)

The idea in words

Output = bend(weighted sum + offset). With many inputs the weighted sum is the dot product from Unit 1 — "multiply each input by how much it matters, add up" — so a neuron is: dot product, plus bias, then bend.

Show the math
a = g(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b)
In plain English The neuron's output (its activation, a) is: the bending function g, applied to (weights dot inputs, plus bias).

    The popular bends, for reference: ReLU(z) = max(0, z) — off below zero, a ramp above; tanh(z) — a smooth S from −1 to +1; sigmoid — the 0-to-1 S you know. Modern networks mostly use ReLU and its relatives: crude, cheap, and its slope doesn't fade at the extremes the way the S-curves' do.
