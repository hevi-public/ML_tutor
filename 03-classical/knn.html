k-nearest neighbors: just ask the locals

The simplest classifier that actually works — no training, no equations, no boundary. It remembers everything and, when asked, polls the neighborhood.

Before you start Vectors — "nearest" means distance, and distance between data points is distance between vectors.

The idea

You find an unfamiliar animal photo. Your instinct: compare it to animals you know — it most resembles a few cats you've seen, so, probably a cat. That entire instinct is the algorithm. k-nearest neighbors (kNN) classifies a new point by finding the k most similar known examples and letting them vote.

Note what's missing: there is no training step. The "model" is the dataset, kept whole. All the work happens at question time — measure the distance to every stored example, take the k closest, count the votes. Slow to answer, instant to "train", and surprisingly strong: with enough data, the locals usually know.

The math (open as much as you like)

The idea in words

"Similar" = "nearby," and nearby is measured with the straight-line distance you learned as Pythagoras: square the gap in each feature, add, square-root. Then: sort by distance, keep k, majority wins.

Show the math
d(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (a_i - b_i)^2}
In plain English The distance between two data points: for each feature, take the gap, square it; add them all; square-root the total. (With two features this is exactly Pythagoras; with a hundred it's the same recipe, unbothered.)

    One practical landmine hides in this formula: it adds raw gaps. If one feature is "age in years" (gaps of ~10) and another is "income in euros" (gaps of ~10,000), income drowns age completely — the vote is rigged by units. The fix is old news to you: rescale features first (subtract the mean, divide by σ). kNN without scaling is the classic beginner faceplant.
