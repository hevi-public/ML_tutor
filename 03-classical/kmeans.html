k-means: when nobody gives you the answers

Your first model with no answer key. Just dots, a hunch about how many groups there are, and a two-step dance that sorts them out.

Before you start kNN — same notion of distance, opposite situation: there we had labels and asked neighbors; here nobody has a label at all.

The idea

A shop owner has data on a thousand customers but no categories — nobody labeled anyone "bargain hunter" or "big spender". The question isn't "predict the label" (there are none); it's "do these customers form natural groups?" Finding groups in unlabeled data is called clustering, and k-means is its classic recipe.

You supply one hunch: k, the number of groups to look for. The algorithm places k guess-centers, then repeats two steps that you can fully understand in one sentence each:

  1. Color the dots: every dot joins its nearest center.
  2. Move the centers: every center relocates to the middle of the dots that joined it (that middle is its centroid — just the mean, feature by feature).

Repeat until no dot wants to switch. That's the entire algorithm — no gradients, no loss curves, just musical chairs settling down.

(2) Set k=5 on this clearly-3-group data — k-means will dutifully shatter real groups to satisfy your bad hunch. The algorithm never questions k; choosing it is your job.

The math (open as much as you like)

The idea in words

k-means is secretly minimizing something: the total squared distance from each dot to its center. "Color the dots" lowers it by reassignment; "move the centers" lowers it by relocation. Since the number only ever goes down and can't go below zero, the dance must settle. (Watch the scatter readout while stepping — it never rises.)

Show the math
J = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left\lVert \mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{c_i} \right\rVert^2
In plain English The score being minimized: for every dot, the squared distance to its own group's center — all added up. Both steps of the dance can only shrink J, which is why the dance always ends.

    Familiar shape, isn't it? Sum of squared distances — the same "squared misses" pattern as MSE and variance. One toolbox, many hats: this time there's no truth to miss, so we measure scatter around our own centers instead.
