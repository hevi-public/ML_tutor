Unit 6 · Modern ML

From predictor to assistant

Pretraining builds a mind-bogglingly knowledgeable text predictor with no manners and no job description. Three add-on stages give it both.

Before you start Large language models — this page is about what happens AFTER that machine exists.

The problem with a raw predictor

Ask a freshly pretrained model "How do I make bread?" and a perfectly correct continuation is "How do I make pasta? How do I make rice?" — because on the internet, questions travel in packs. The model isn't broken; it's answering the question it was actually trained on ("what text comes next?"), not the one you meant ("help me"). Closing that gap is called alignment, and it takes a tiny fraction of the compute that pretraining did — a finishing school, not a second education.

The three stages

  1. Supervised fine-tuning. Continue training — same backprop, same log loss — on a small, curated set of example dialogues written the way an assistant should respond. Unit 2 vocabulary: it's just more training data, chosen to demonstrate the job. After this, the model answers questions instead of continuing them.
  2. Preference tuning (RLHF and relatives). Some qualities are easier to recognize than to demonstrate: which of two answers is clearer? more honest? People rank pairs of model outputs; a reward model is trained to predict those rankings (a grader — a learned loss, really); then the assistant is tuned to score well on it. That's "reinforcement learning from human feedback": learning from grades instead of worked examples.
  3. A system prompt. The cheapest layer: standing instructions prepended invisibly to every conversation ("You are a helpful assistant. Decline harmful requests…"). No training at all — just context the predictor conditions on.

Prompting vs. fine-tuning: which lever when?

Once assistants existed, a new question appeared: to make the model do your task, do you change its input or its weights?

  • Prompting — change the input. Describe the task, show a few examples in the prompt itself ("few-shot"), paste in reference documents. Zero training, instant iteration, and the model's weights never move — remarkably, a good prompt with three examples often matches what used to require a training run. Start here, always.
  • Fine-tuning — change the weights. Worth it when the task needs a consistent style at high volume, knowledge too large to paste into every prompt, or a small cheap model made expert at one narrow thing. Costs: training runs, evaluation discipline, and the old Unit 2 risks — overfit your 500 examples and the model gets worse at everything else.
  • The middle path that ate the industry: retrieval — embed your documents (Unit 5!), fetch the relevant ones per question, paste them into the prompt. Fresh knowledge, no training, sources you can cite.

How do you grade a generated paragraph?

A quiet crisis behind all of this: Unit 2's metrics assumed a right answer to compare against. Generated text has no single right answer. Current practice is a patchwork worth knowing by name: benchmark suites (exam-style question sets — with a chronic worry that their questions leak into training data), human side-by-side preferences (the leaderboards), and LLM-as-judge — using a strong model to grade another's output, cheap but with obvious circularity risks. Evaluation is genuinely unsolved, and anyone quoting a single number for "how good is this model" is simplifying.
