Diffusion: images out of static

The trick behind modern image generators sounds like a joke: teach a network to remove noise, then hand it pure noise and let it "remove" its way to a picture that never existed.

Before you start Convolutions (the denoiser is a CNN-flavored network) and the general idea that networks learn by example. This is the dessert course — enjoy it.

The idea

Generating an image from scratch is monstrous: every pixel depends on every other. Diffusion breaks the monster into a thousand trivial steps, via a training scheme of ruthless cleverness:

  1. Wreck things (no learning needed). Take a real photo, add a little random static. Add a little more. Repeat until pure noise. Do this to millions of photos, keeping every intermediate stage.
  2. Learn to un-wreck slightly. Train a network on: given a partly-noised image (and how noisy it is), predict the noise that was added — so it can be subtracted back out. Just supervised learning! The "label" is the exact noise you yourself added one step ago. Data and labels, manufactured for free, in any quantity.
  3. Run it backwards to create. Start from pure random static and apply the denoiser step by step. Each step, it nudges the pixels toward "what a real image would look like if this were its noisy version." A thousand nudges later: a coherent picture — of nothing that ever existed, because the starting static was random.

Find the dial position where the picture stops being recognizable to YOU (~60–75%?). A trained denoiser keeps recovering structure well past where your eye gives up — at every noise level, faint statistical echoes of the image survive, and it has seen millions of examples of exactly what they look like. Honesty note: this demo knows the original picture, so its walk-back is a re-blend. The real model does NOT know — it must hallucinate a plausible image consistent with the static, which is precisely why generation from fresh static yields fresh pictures.

The pieces you've already met, reassembled

  • The denoiser is just a network — convolution-heavy (local texture) with attention layers (global coherence), trained with backprop on a plain "predicted noise vs. actual noise" squared-error loss. Unit 3's MSE, moving billions of pixels.
  • Text control is embeddings, again. "A cat in a space suit" is embedded (Unit 5) and fed into the denoiser at every step, so each nudge leans toward images that match the caption. Same trick as everywhere: shared vector space, geometry does the work.
  • Contrast with LLMs: language models build their output one token at a time, left to right; diffusion refines the WHOLE image at once, coarse to fine — global layout emerges in the noisiest steps, texture detail in the last ones. Two different ways of taming \"everything depends on everything\".
