What is machine learning?

You already know how to do this. You've just never called it that.

You've been doing it all along

Think about how you learned to guess whether it'll rain from the look of the sky. Nobody gave you a rulebook. You saw a lot of skies, noticed what followed, and your guesses got better. The "rule" in your head — dark and heavy in the west means bring an umbrella — was never written down. It grew out of examples.

That is the whole trick. Ordinary programming is writing the rule down: a person types "IF the sky is dark THEN predict rain," and the computer obeys. Machine learning flips it: we show the computer lots of examples with known outcomes, and it works out a rule of its own — by adjusting its internal settings until its guesses match the examples well.

The thing being adjusted is called a model: a guessing machine with tunable settings. Training is the adjustment process. That's the vocabulary — everything else on this site is detail, and each detail gets its own page.

The two big families

Most of what you'll meet falls into one of two situations:

  • Learning with an answer key — every example comes with the right answer attached: this email was spam, this house did sell for $400k. The computer practices and gets graded. This is called supervised learning, and it covers two kinds of question: predict a number (regression), or predict a category (classification).
  • Learning without an answer key — just a pile of data, and the job is to find structure in it: which customers behave alike? This is unsupervised learning.

Modern AI — chatbots, image generators — is built on the same foundations, with more layers of cleverness stacked on top. By Unit 6 you'll be able to see the line from "fit a straight line through dots" to "write me a poem."

What ML is not

A model is not smart, and it doesn't "understand." It's a settings-tuning process that makes a guessing machine agree with past examples. That has two practical consequences worth carrying with you from day one:

  • A model is only as good as its examples. Feed it lopsided data and it learns lopsided rules, confidently.
  • Agreeing with past examples isn't the same as being right about new ones — a model can just memorize the past. This gap has a name (overfitting), a whole page later, and it's the single most important idea in practical ML.
