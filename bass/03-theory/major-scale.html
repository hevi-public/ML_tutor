The major scale — music's measuring stick

Do–re–mi is not a children's song, it's the coordinate system. Chords, keys, other scales, even "wrong notes" are all described by how they differ from this one seven-note recipe.

Before you start Whole and half steps (two frets / one fret) and interval names.

The idea

The major scale is a recipe of steps: Whole, Whole, Half, Whole, Whole, Whole, Half — in frets: 2 · 2 · 1 · 2 · 2 · 2 · 1. Start on any note, apply the recipe, and you get that note's major scale. Start on C and it happens to use only natural notes (C D E F G A B); start anywhere else and sharps or flats appear because the recipe demands them, not by decoration.

Each note gets a number — its degree: 1 2 3 4 5 6 7, then 1 again (the octave). Those numbers are how musicians talk: "root and 5", "walk up to the 3", "flat 7". Learn the numbers with your hands now and every future page gets easier.

Why those two half steps matter

The half steps fall between degrees 3–4 and 7–8. Degree 7 sits one semitone under home and leans into it (play 7 then stop — feel the itch). Degree 4 leans down onto 3. These two gravitational spots are why melodies resolve where they do — and why the approach notes you'll use in bass lines work.

Degrees have names too

1 tonic (home) · 2 supertonic · 3 mediant · 4 subdominant · 5 dominant (the other pole) · 6 submediant · 7 leading tone (the leaner). Bassists mostly say the numbers — but "tonic" and "dominant" appear on every theory page ever written, including the next few here.

Two positions for the same scale

The box shown starts with finger 2 on the root (E- or A-string). A second essential fingering starts with finger 4 — same notes, hand one position lower. Having both means the scale is always under your hand, whichever way the line is heading.

Practice

Scales are for saying numbers with your hands — always count degrees out loud, never just run fingers.
