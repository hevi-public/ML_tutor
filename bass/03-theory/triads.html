Triads — the atoms of harmony

Every chord chart you'll ever read is mostly these: three notes, stacked in thirds. The band plays them together; you'll play them apart — that's what a bass line mostly is.

Before you start Thirds and fifths as sounds and shapes.

The idea

A triad = root + third + fifth. The fifth is scaffolding (perfect, stable); the third picks the mood. Mix and match the two third-sizes and two fifth-sizes and you get exactly four flavors:

TriadRecipe (semitones)SymbolCharacter
Majorroot + 4 + 3 (M3 then m3)Cbright, settled
Minorroot + 3 + 4Cm, C−dark, settled
Diminishedroot + 3 + 3C°, Cdimclenched, unstable
Augmentedroot + 4 + 4C+, Caugfloating, eerie

Played one note at a time, a triad is an arpeggio — and arpeggios are the most chord-proof material a bass line can be made of: you literally cannot clash with the chord while spelling it.

Build one, hear it, walk it

The one-position arpeggio shapes

From a root on the E or A string, both essential flavors sit under one hand: major = root (finger 2), third one string up one fret back (1), fifth one string up two frets over (4). Minor = same but the third slides one more fret back. Practice below burns these in — they're the skeleton keys of Unit 5.

The details (open as much as you like)

Where triads come from (stacking the scale)

Take a scale, keep every other note: degrees 1-3-5. Do it starting from each degree and the scale generates seven triads — its private chord family. That factory (and its Roman-numeral naming) is the diatonic harmony page, two stops from here.

Why diminished sounds "clenched"

Squash the fifth from 7 semitones to 6 and you've built a tritone into the chord — the itchiest interval, demanding resolution. Diminished triads are doors, not rooms: music passes through them. (Augmented stretches the fifth to 8 and floats free of gravity instead — rarer, dreamier.)
