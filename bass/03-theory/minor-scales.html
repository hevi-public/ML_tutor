Minor scales

Most of the music you love that isn't sunshine lives here. The good news: you already own the notes — minor is the major scale wearing different gravity.

Before you start The major scale and relative keys (the circle's inner ring).

The idea

Take C major's seven notes but make A feel like home instead of C, and everything changes character: same palette, darker painting. That's the natural minor scale — and every minor scale is some major scale's relative, rooted three semitones below it (A minor ↔ C major, E minor ↔ G major…).

Measured from its own root, natural minor's recipe is 2 · 1 · 2 · 2 · 1 · 2 · 2, and in degree language it's the major scale with ♭3, ♭6, ♭7 — three notes shifted one fret down. The ♭3 alone flips the mood; you heard exactly this on the thirds page.

The details (open as much as you like)

Relative vs parallel — two ways to "go minor"

Relative minor shares notes but moves home (C major ↔ A minor — zero accidentals change). Parallel minor keeps the home and changes notes (C major ↔ C minor — flat the 3rd, 6th, 7th). Charts and the circle think in relatives; your ear at a jam usually notices parallels ("same root, sadder"). Keep both words — they answer different questions.

Harmonic & melodic minor (the famous tweaks)

Natural minor's ♭7 doesn't lean into home the way major's leading tone does. Classical harmony fixes it by raising the 7th — harmonic minor — creating that instantly-recognizable exotic gap between ♭6 and 7. Raising the 6th as well smooths the melody — melodic minor. For now: recognize the names, know why they exist (stronger pull to the root), and file them for the Expert unit. Rock, pop, and funk live overwhelmingly in natural minor (and its pentatonic — next page).

Minor key signatures

A minor key borrows its relative major's signature: E minor wears G major's one sharp. When a chart says "1 sharp", the tonic is either G (major) or E (minor) — the first bass note and the resting chord usually settle which. The circle page's inner ring is exactly this lookup.
