Diatonic harmony — a key's chord family

Why do certain chords "belong together"? Because a key manufactures its own chords — seven of them, with fixed personalities and numbered name tags. Learn the tags and you can hear a song once and know what the bass should do.

Before you start Scale degrees, triads, and seventh qualities.

The idea

Build a triad (1-3-5 stack) on each degree of a major scale, using only the scale's own notes — diatonic means exactly that. The spacing of the scale forces each chord's quality; nobody chooses it:

I major · ii minor · iii minor · IV major · V major · vi minor · vii diminished

Written as Roman numerals (uppercase = major, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished), the pattern is the same in every key — which is why musicians say "a I–V–vi–IV" instead of naming four chords twelve different ways. Session players' Nashville numbers are the same idea with digits: 1 5 6− 4.

The details (open as much as you like)

Why V pulls home so hard

The V chord contains the key's leading tone (degree 7, a semitone under home) — and as a V7 it adds the 4, forming a tritone that must resolve. Both tension notes collapse inward onto the I chord. Your root motion V→I underlines the strongest gravity harmony has; that's why cadences live or die by the bass.

Minor keys run the same factory

Natural minor generates: i · ii° · III · iv · v · VI · VII. Same machine, rotated (compare: it's the relative major's family starting from vi). In practice minor-key music often borrows a major V from harmonic minor for that leading-tone pull — you'll hear both flavors in the styles unit.

What this buys you at a jam

Someone shouts "it's a 1-4-5 in G!" and you now know every chord before the count-in: G, C, D. Numbers + the circle of fifths + movable shapes = you can play songs you've never heard in keys you didn't choose. That's not a party trick; it's the actual job.
