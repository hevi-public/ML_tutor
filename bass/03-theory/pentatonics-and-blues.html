Pentatonics & the blues scale

Remove a scale's two riskiest notes and what's left can hardly sound wrong. Five notes power more famous bass lines than the other seven-note theory combined.

Before you start Major and minor scales as degree numbers.

The idea

The minor pentatonic is natural minor minus its two half-step-adjacent notes (the 2 and ♭6): 1 · ♭3 · 4 · 5 · ♭7. No note sits a semitone from any chord tone, so nothing clashes hard — which is why you can lean on any of these five over a groove and sound intentional. The major pentatonic plays the same trick on the major scale: 1 · 2 · 3 · 5 · 6 (drop the 4 and 7) — instant country, gospel, and Motown sweetness.

Add one spicy chromatic note to the minor pentatonic — the ♭5 — and you have the blues scale: 1 · ♭3 · 4 · ♭5 · 5 · ♭7. The ♭5 is a passing sting, not a home: slide through it and it's the blues; sit on it and it's a horror film.

Why five notes feel so safe (one layer deeper)

The notes removed are precisely the ones that form semitone rubs against chord tones: the 4 sits on the 3's shoulder, the 7 leans on the root, minor's 2 and ♭6 crowd the ♭3 and 5. What remains keeps a polite whole-step-or-more distance from trouble. Less tension available — but total permission to move freely. That trade IS the pentatonic sound.

Relative pentatonics — one shape, two jobs

A minor pentatonic and C major pentatonic contain the identical five notes (relatives again!). So the box you learn today is also your major pentatonic — just anchored on a different root dot. This is the only-learn-it-once economy that makes pentatonics such good value.

Bassists vs guitarists, pentatonically

Guitarists solo with this scale; bassists build with it. The classic rock/soul bass vocabulary — root, ♭7 slide, 4-to-5 walk, ♭3 hammer — is pentatonic geometry under a groove. When Unit 5 constructs lines, most connective tissue will come from this page.
