Keys & the circle of fifths

"What key is it in?" is the first question at every jam. The answer names the home note, the seven-note palette, and — via one elegant circle — how every key relates to every other.

Before you start The major scale and its recipe; the fifth as an interval.

The idea

A key is a home base plus its major (or minor) scale: "in G major" means G feels like home and the music draws from G's seven notes. Rather than re-flagging F♯ on every line, written music declares it once — the key signature after the clef.

Now the beautiful part. Walk from C up a perfect fifth to G, and G's scale differs from C's by exactly one note (F becomes F♯). Up another fifth to D: one more sharp. Every fifth you climb adds one sharp; every fifth you descend adds one flat. Twelve steps of this and you're back home — a closed circle.

The circle, live

Tap any key — outer ring majors, inner ring their relative minors. It plays the scale and shows the signature.

Neighbors on the circle share six of seven notes — that's why songs drift to them so easily, and why the two chords next to your key (IV and V!) feel like family.

Reading a key signature at a glance

  • Sharps: the last sharp is the leading tone — go one semitone up from it and you've named the key. (Two sharps, last is C♯ → key of D.)
  • Flats: the second-to-last flat IS the key. (Three flats B♭-E♭-A♭ → E♭ major.) With one flat, memorize it: F.
  • The sharps always arrive in the order F-C-G-D-A-E-B; flats in the exact reverse. Nothing to compute — it's the circle again.

The details (open as much as you like)

What the circle does for a bassist, concretely

Three jobs: (1) Instant key-signature lookup. (2) The notes either side of your key are its IV and V — the two most common chords in the language, pre-located. (3) Root motion by fifths (C→F→B♭…) is the strongest sound in harmony; countless progressions and the entire ii–V–I move counter-clockwise along this circle. Practicing anything "around the circle" therefore practices the motion real songs use.

Enharmonic seams

At the bottom of the circle, spelling flips: F♯ major (6 sharps) and G♭ major (6 flats) are the same sounds written differently. Writers pick whichever spelling reads easier. Your hands don't care — same frets.

Why fifths generate everything

Stack twelve perfect fifths and you visit all twelve pitch classes exactly once before returning (7 semitones and 12 notes share no common factor — musical number theory). That's why the circle exists at all, and why the fifth feels like music's load-bearing interval.
