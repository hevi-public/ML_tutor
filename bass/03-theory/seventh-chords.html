Seventh chords & how charts spell them

Stack one more third on a triad and harmony grows up: blues, soul, funk, and jazz are written almost entirely in these four-note chords — and in the shorthand this page teaches you to read.

Before you start Triads — sevenths are triads plus one decision.

The idea

A seventh chord adds the 7th degree above the root. Like the third, the seventh comes in two sizes (major 7 = 11 semitones, minor/"flat" 7 = 10) — and the combination of triad + seventh size gives the four workhorses:

ChordBuildWrittenPersonality
Major sevenmajor triad + M7Cmaj7, CΔ7dreamy, floating, "jazz ballad"
Dominant sevenmajor triad + ♭7C7bluesy, restless, wants to move
Minor sevenminor triad + ♭7Cm7, C−7mellow, warm, soul's default
Half-diminisheddim triad + ♭7Cø7, Cm7♭5tense, noir, minor keys' ii

Beware the classic trap: C7 and Cmaj7 are different chords. A plain 7 always means the bluesy ♭7; "maj7"/Δ marks the dreamy natural 7. Their sevenths sit one fret apart and one world apart.

Hear the four flavors

Reading chord symbols — the survival grammar

  • Letter = root (your note!). Alone = major triad.
  • m / − / min = minor third. maj / Δ before a 7 = major seventh.
  • A number (7, 9, 13) = that degree stacked on; plain numbers imply the ♭7 underneath.
  • ° dim, ø, + aug, sus = the triad modifications you've met.
  • Slash (C/E) = chord left of the slash, bass note right of it — the one symbol that's literally an instruction to you personally.

The details (open as much as you like)

What the bassist actually plays over sevenths

Roots and fifths still pay the rent. The upgrade sevenths offer is the ♭7 as a melodic hook (the R–♭7 rock/soul move) and the 3rd to signal quality in walking lines. You almost never need to sound all four notes — you imply them, one tasty tone at a time. Unit 5 does exactly this, at length.

Where each flavor lives in a key

Preview of the next page: build sevenths on every degree of a major scale and the flavors sort themselves — maj7 on I and IV, dominant 7 on V (only!), m7 on ii, iii, vi, ø7 on vii. That's why hearing "…m7 …7 …maj7" in a chart practically announces ii–V–I before you've found the key.
