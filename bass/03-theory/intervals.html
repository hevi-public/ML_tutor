Intervals — distances with personalities

You already play interval shapes. This page attaches the proper names and — much more useful — the sounds, so "a fifth" stops being geometry and becomes something you recognize with your eyes closed.

Before you start Interval shapes on the neck (Unit 2) and counting frets as semitones.

The idea

Every interval has a two-part name: a number (how many letter names you cross, counting both ends: C→E crosses C, D, E = a third) and a quality (major, minor, perfect — the fine-tuning). The everyday translation:

The details (open as much as you like)

Why "perfect"? Why "major/minor"?

Unisons, fourths, fifths, and octaves are so acoustically stable that old theorists called them perfect — they don't come in happy/sad flavors. Seconds, thirds, sixths, and sevenths do: the larger version is major, one semitone smaller is minor. That's the entire naming system. (Shrink a perfect interval and it's diminished; stretch it, augmented — the tritone is both an augmented fourth and a diminished fifth, which suits its troublemaker reputation.)

Consonance and dissonance — the working bassist's version

Octaves and fifths sound "at rest" against the root — that's why they carry whole genres of bass playing. Thirds and sixths sound sweet; seconds and sevenths rub; the tritone actively demands resolution. None of these are good or bad — they're tools with different urgencies, and bass lines are built by choosing when to be stable and when to itch.

Compound intervals (past the octave)

A ninth is just a second plus an octave, an eleventh a fourth plus an octave, a thirteenth a sixth. You'll meet them as chord extensions in the Expert unit — the ear-math is the same, only wider.
