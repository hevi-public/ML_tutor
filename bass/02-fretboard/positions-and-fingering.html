Unit 2 · The Fretboard

Positions & fingering

Your four fingers can own four neighboring frets without moving your hand. Decide once which finger owns which fret, and most "where do I put my fingers?" questions answer themselves forever.

Before you start Interval shapes (previous page) — positions are where shapes live.

The idea

Park your hand so your index finger hovers over, say, fret 5. Then: index = fret 5, middle = 6, ring = 7, pinky = 8. That claim — one finger per fret — defines a position (this one is "5th position", named after the index finger's fret). Within a position you can reach every note in a 4-fret × 4-string window — around 25 notes — without your hand traveling an inch.

See the window

Numbers show which finger owns each fret: 1 = index … 4 = pinky. Tap any marked fret to hear it.

The rules of thumb (literally)

  • Thumb behind the neck, roughly opposite your middle finger, pointing up-ish. It's a pivot, not a clamp.
  • Fingers stay curled and close to the strings — hovering a centimeter away, not flying up between notes.
  • Press with fingertips just behind the fret, as you learned in setup — the position system tells you which tip.
  • Near the nut, relax the rule: frets 1–4 are wide, so many players cover them index-middle-pinky (1-2-4, the "three-fret" fingering borrowed from upright bass). Both systems are correct; big stretches are not.

Shifting: moving house gracefully

Shifts — moving the whole hand to a new position — are planned, not panicked. The pros' tricks: shift during an open string or a rest; lighten the thumb and glide (don't hop); and lead with the finger that will play the next note, arriving just as it's needed. The last exercise below drills exactly this.

The details (open as much as you like)

Why bother with a system at all?

Because hesitation is the enemy of time. When every note in the window has exactly one finger assigned, your hands stop making decisions mid-bar — and decision-free hands are what "effortless" players actually have. The system also makes written fingerings (the little 1-2-3-4 numbers under notes) instantly meaningful.

Same lick, three positions

Most phrases fit in several windows. A C major arpeggio: around 3rd position using the A-string C; around 7th position using the E-string C (fret 8); or open-position mixing open strings. They sound nearly identical — they set up differently for what comes next. Choosing positions by "where am I going?" is the skill; it starts by knowing more than one option exists.

Practice

The chromatic 1-2-3-4 drill looks robotic and is quietly the most valuable warmup on the instrument — it trains the finger-per-fret reflex, evenness, and string crossing at once.
