Unit 2 · The Fretboard

Notes on the fretboard

The neck looks like a hundred anonymous places to put your fingers. It's actually a small, repeating map — and you only need a few landmarks to never feel lost on it again.

Before you start You should be in tune (tuning page) and know that your strings, low to high, are E–A–D–G.

The idea

Music uses just twelve note names, then they repeat. Seven of them are the plain letters A–G, called the natural notes; the other five live between them and are named with ♯ (one fret up) or ♭ (one fret down).

On a bass, the map is dead simple: each fret is one semitone. Start on any open string and walk up one fret at a time and you pass through all twelve names; at the 12th fret you're back where you started, one octave higher. That's why the 12th fret has a double dot — it's the "you've gone all the way around" marker.

Find it

Tap any fret to hear it and see its name.

Sound starts with your first tap. Notice how every C sits in the same visual relationship to its neighbors — shapes repeat everywhere.

The landmarks

Don't memorize 48 positions. Learn a few anchors and derive the rest:

  • Open strings: E, A, D, G. Free notes, no fingers.
  • Fret 3 on the A string is C. The single most useful note to know — huge amounts of music live around it.
  • Fret 5 replays the next open string. E-string fret 5 is A; A-string fret 5 is D; D-string fret 5 is G. (That's also how you cross-check your tuning.)
  • Fret 12 = the open string, an octave up. Double dot, same name.
  • The dots at 3 · 5 · 7 · 9 · 12 are your GPS — with them you never have to count frets from the nut.

The octave shape

The single most useful shape on the bass: from any note, go two strings up and two frets over and you land on the same note an octave higher. Press "An octave pair" above to see one. This shape turns every note you learn into two notes — learn the E and A strings well and the octave shape hands you the D and G strings for free.

The details (open as much as you like)

The idea in words

Between most neighboring letters there's one in-between note (C♯/D♭ between C and D). Two pairs are special: B–C and E–F are direct neighbors — no note between them, they sit on adjacent frets. Every "why is that note there?!" moment on the neck traces back to those two pairs.

Why the same note has two names

The note between C and D is called C♯ when you're thinking "one above C" and D♭ when you're thinking "one below D". Same fret, same sound. Which name gets used depends on the key you're in — that story is told properly in Keys & the circle of fifths (Unit 3). For now: two names, one place.

Why 12 frets, exactly?

An octave up means the string vibrates exactly twice as fast. Western music slices that doubling into 12 equal steps (each fret multiplies the pitch by 21/12 ≈ 1.059 — about 6% faster per fret). Twelve steps of 6% compound to exactly 2× — which is also why the frets get visibly narrower as you go up the neck.

Practice

Say every note name out loud as you play it — the point isn't moving a finger up a string, it's welding names to places. Slow enough that you never guess.
