Unit 2 · The Fretboard

Strings & fret math

Four strings, tuned a fixed distance apart, sliced by frets into equal steps. Three facts — and the entire neck becomes a system instead of a mystery.

Before you start Your strings are in tune (tuning) and you know one fret = one semitone.

Fact 1 — the strings are all a fourth apart

E → A → D → G: each string sits exactly five semitones (five frets' worth) above its thicker neighbor. That regular spacing is a gift — every pattern you learn works across ALL string pairs, because the relationship between neighbors never changes. (Guitarists aren't so lucky; their B string breaks the pattern.)

Fact 2 — fret 5 duplicates the next string

Direct consequence: press fret 5 and you've recreated the next open string. Tap around and prove it:

Tap the buttons — or any fret — to hear it.

Same note, two homes. This is why the same lick can be played in several places, and why your tuning cross-check works.

Fact 3 — every fret multiplies by the same tiny amount

Each fret raises the string's vibration speed by about 6% (precisely: ×21/12 ≈ 1.0595). Do that twelve times and you've exactly doubled the frequency — which your ear hears as "the same note, higher": the octave at fret 12. Equal steps are also why the frets get physically narrower up the neck: each one cuts off 6% of the remaining string length.

In plain English Frets are a ruler marked in equal musical steps. Twelve steps = one octave = double the vibration speed. The narrowing spacing isn't decoration — it's the 6% rule made visible.

The details (open as much as you like)

Why tune in fourths at all?

Five frets is what a hand can comfortably span. Tuned this way, one hand position reaches a two-octave patchwork of notes without moving — wider tuning would mean stretches, narrower would mean constant shifting. Fourths are the ergonomic sweet spot, which is why the bass, the double bass before it, and most of the guitar all settled there.

The numbers, one layer deeper

Open E vibrates ≈ 41.2 times per second (41.2 Hz). Fret 1 (F): 41.2 × 1.0595 ≈ 43.7 Hz. Fret 12: 41.2 × 2 = 82.4 Hz — the octave E. Every note on the instrument comes from that one multiplication, repeated. (The general recipe: frequency = open-string frequency × 2frets/12.)

Where this breaks (and why nobody panics)

Pressing a string stretches it slightly sharp, and real strings aren't ideal mathematical strings. That's what a setup's intonation adjustment compensates for — moving each bridge saddle until the 12th fret really is a perfect octave. Math proposes; the tech disposes.
