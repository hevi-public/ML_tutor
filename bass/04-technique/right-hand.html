Unit 4 · Technique

The right hand — where your sound is born

Ninety percent of "that bassist sounds amazing" is the plucking hand: evenness, note length, and quiet strings. It's built from three boring habits, practiced until they're invisible.

Before you start Anchoring and posture from gear & setup. (Left-handed players: mirror everything — "right hand" here means the plucking hand.)

The three habits

1 — Strict alternation. Index, middle, index, middle (fingerstyle). No favorites, no doubling when nervous. Alternation is what makes fast passages possible later — and it only becomes automatic if it's absolute now, at slow tempos.

2 — The rest stroke. Pluck through the string so your finger lands on the next-thicker string (rest stroke). Fat tone and free muting in one motion — the landed finger silences the string it touches.

3 — Everything else stays quiet. The thumb rides the pickup or the E string; idle fingers and the fretting hand's underside damp what isn't speaking. Muting isn't a skill you add later — it IS the technique.

Where to pluck

You chose a spot on the setup page by ear; now make it a decision per song: near the bridge = tight, defined, cuts through (tone for busy lines); near the neck = deep, round, huge (great for slow roots); in between = the everyday default. Moving your hand is a free EQ pedal.

The details (open as much as you like)

How hard to pluck (softer than you think)

Digging in constantly costs evenness and speed, and the amp can be loud for you. Practice at a firm-but-relaxed medium and save the dig for accents — dynamics are only available to players who don't live at maximum. A useful check: your plucking fingers should never feel tired before your brain does.

Raking — the legal shortcut

Descending across strings, let one finger fall through both (raking) instead of forcing alternation. G-string then D-string with one finger's sweep is smoother AND quieter than two separate plucks. Alternation is the rule; raking is its officially sanctioned exception.

Floating thumb (for later, or for 5-strings)

Instead of a fixed anchor, some players rest the thumb on whichever string is below the one speaking — it mutes as it travels. Worth adopting when you add a low B string or when E-string rumble bothers your recordings. File it; don't fight it now.

Practice

Slow is the whole point: these drills build the automatic machine. Uneven at 60 becomes unfixable at 120.
