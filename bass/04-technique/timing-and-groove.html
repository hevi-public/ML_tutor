Timing & groove — the actual job

Nobody hires a bassist for fast fingers. They hire the feeling that the floor is solid. This page is how that feeling is built: a metronome, subdivision, and honest listening.

Before you start Counting out loud — this page turns counting into feel.

The idea

Solid time isn't hitting beats like a machine — it's continuously feeling the subdivision so every note lands where you chose, not where your nerves put it. The metronome isn't a judge; it's a drummer who never rushes. Your relationship with it goes through three stages: playing to it, playing with it, and finally hearing it disappear because you're locked (in the pocket).

The habits that make time solid

  • Subdivide out loud (or in your chest, later). Long notes drift when nothing is counting through them.
  • Place, don't react. Aim each note at the click; don't wait to hear the click and chase it.
  • Record yourself weekly. Twenty seconds of phone audio tells the truth about rushing that your in-the-moment brain hides.
  • Note length is part of rhythm. Where a note ends grooves as much as where it starts — you built that control on the right-hand page.

The classic drills, explained

Half-time click: set the metronome to half your tempo and hear it as beats 2 and 4 (the snare drum) — instantly exposes rushing. Gap practice: mute the click every other bar (our groove machine can); if you're off when it returns, the drift is yours. One-click-per-bar: the endgame — you keep the whole bar's time yourself.

Ahead, behind, and "on top" — feel vocabulary

Great rhythm sections place notes a hair early (urgent, punk), dead-center (neutral, pop), or a hair late (relaxed, soul) — consistently, by choice. That's "feel". It's only available once dead-center is effortless; the drills here buy you the choice.
