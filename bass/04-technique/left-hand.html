Unit 4 · Technique

The left hand — clean, light, and legato

The fretting hand's job description is shorter than people think: arrive early, press lightly, leave cleanly. Plus two tricks — hammer-ons and pull-offs — that turn effortful lines into flowing ones.

Before you start Positions & one-finger-per-fret — this page is about making that system sound good.

Pressure: the eye-opener

Try this now: fret any note, pluck repeatedly, and slowly release pressure until the note buzzes — then press just enough to clean it up. That's the required force. Most self-taught players use three times more, and pay for it in speed, stamina, and sore hands. Light touch isn't a style; it's physics.

Hammer-ons & pull-offs

Two notes, one pluck: hammer-on (snap a finger down onto a higher fret — written h in tab) and its mirror the pull-off (flick the finger off sideways so the lower note speaks — written p). Together with slides, they're how bass lines get that vocal, connected (legato) quality — and how fast figures stop costing plucks.

The silent skills

  • Finger independence — each finger moves without its neighbors flinching. The permutation drill below is the classic builder.
  • Fretting-hand muting — the underside of your fingers lies gently across higher strings; releasing pressure (without leaving the string) stops notes. Half of funk's crispness is this.
  • Economy of motion — fingers hover a centimeter from the strings. Watch any great player: the hands look lazy. That's the goal.

The details (open as much as you like)

Where exactly behind the fret?

As close behind the metal as comfortable — pressure needed drops dramatically there. In one-finger-per-fret position your index and pinky can't both be perfect; prioritize whichever is playing. "Just behind the fret" beats "middle of the box" every time you can afford it.

Hammer/pull mechanics that actually work

Hammer-ons are a snap from the knuckle, not a push — speed makes the note, not force. Pull-offs are a tiny sideways pluck by the leaving finger (straight lifting gives silence). Both need the lower note's finger already planted before the move. Slow reps with a loud, clear second note beat fast mushy ones by a mile.

Thumb pain checklist

If the fretting hand aches: neck angle too flat (raise it), thumb clamping (remember: the strap holds the bass, not your hand), wrist bent sharply (straighten by adjusting elbow), or pressing too hard (redo the pressure test). Pain is data, never a rite of passage.
