Unit 4 · Technique

How to practice

The difference between two years of progress and two years of repeating year one is not talent or hours — it's method. This page is the method, and it fits on an index card.

The index card

The whole method 1. One specific goal per session ("clean shift in bar 3", not "get better").
2. Slow enough to be perfect; the metronome decides, not your mood.
3. Loop the smallest broken piece, not the whole song.
4. Raise the tempo only after 3 clean repeats; drop back two notches after every miss.
5. Short and daily beats long and weekly. Log it (the buttons on every exercise here do this).

Why slow works (it's not about caution)

Every repetition — clean or sloppy — is a vote for what your hands will do automatically. Practicing mistakes at speed literally trains the mistakes. Slow practice is how you cast only good votes; speed then arrives on its own, unnervingly fast. The rule of thumb: if you can't play it perfectly three times in a row, it's too fast to be practice.

Chunking: the loop that fixes things

Never "run it again from the top". Find the two beats that break, loop just those (with one note of lead-in and one of follow-through), fix them, then reattach: bar → phrase → section. Ten minutes of surgical looping beats an hour of hopeful run-throughs — this is the single largest efficiency upgrade available to any self-taught player.

A session shape that works (20–30 min)

  • 2 min — tune (always; it's also ear training).
  • 5 min — hands warmup: the 1-2-3-4 permutations or alternation drill, with the metronome.
  • 10–15 min — the one goal: this week's exercise or song chunk, looped and logged.
  • 5 min — music: play something you love, badly or not. This is why you started; guard it.

The routine builder in the Practice Labs unit assembles this automatically from your practice log and progress.

The details (open as much as you like)

Plateaus and what they mean

Progress is stairs, not a ramp: weeks of nothing, then a jump — usually right after the frustrating stretch. Plateaus are consolidation, not failure. The log matters here: "practiced 9×, last Tuesday" is evidence against the feeling that nothing's happening.

Practicing vs playing (you need both)

Practicing is slow, looped, goal-driven, slightly uncomfortable. Playing is whole songs, flow, joy. Confusing them causes both failure modes: all-play never improves; all-practice quits within a year. The session shape above deliberately ends with play.

When something never gets clean

Three honest checks: Is the tempo REALLY slow enough (halve it)? Is the chunk REALLY small enough (two beats)? Is a technique missing (odd fingering, missing shift — revisit that page)? If all three pass and it still fights you after a week, park it and return in a month — you'll often find it fixed itself while you grew elsewhere.
