Modes — seven flavors of one scale

You met the idea in Unit 3: same notes, different home. Now make it practical — because two of the seven modes (Dorian and Mixolydian) quietly run most of the funk, rock, and jam-band music you play.

Before you start Major scale and relative thinking. From here on, standard terminology leads — every term still taps.

The two ways to think about modes

Derivative (good for finding notes): D Dorian = the C major scale played from D. Parallel (good for hearing): D Dorian = D natural minor with the 6th raised. The first gets your fingers there; the second tells your ears what changed. Experts flip between both without noticing.

The details (open as much as you like)

When modes actually matter to a bassist

Honestly: on vamps and modal tunes — one chord for a long time, where the color notes you choose (the Dorian 6th, the Mixolydian ♭7) ARE the harmony. On fast-changing progressions, chord tones + approach grammar matter far more than mode names. Modes are seasoning for harmonic standstill.

Hearing them fast: the one-note trick

Each daily-driver mode differs from plain major/minor by ONE note: Mixolydian = major with ♭7; Dorian = minor with natural 6; Lydian = major with ♯4; Phrygian = minor with ♭2. Learn to hear those four single notes over a drone and you've got modal ears with four flashcards of effort.
