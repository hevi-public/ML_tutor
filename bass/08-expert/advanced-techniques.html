Advanced techniques — slap, tap, harmonics, and the fifth string

The flashy wing of the vocabulary. Everything here is optional; everything here is fun; none of it substitutes for the pocket you built in Units 4–6 — it decorates it.

Before you start Solid 16th-grid + ghost notes — slap is 80% that skill wearing a leather jacket.

Slap & pop, the honest primer

  • The thumb (slap): strike the string with the side of the thumb's knuckle at the neck's end, bouncing OFF like a drumstick — the fret-clack is the sound. Rotate from the forearm, loose wrist.
  • The finger (pop): index slips under the D or G string and yanks it to snap against the frets. Thumb-pop pairs are funk's snare-and-kick.
  • Ghosts still rule: slap lines are mostly muted thumb/pop on the grid; the pitched notes are the minority. If your funk ghosts are solid, slap is a tone change, not a new skill.
  • Start at 60 bpm, thumb-only on one muted string, even as a metronome. Speed is compound interest on evenness.

Tapping, harmonics, palm+thumb

  • Tapping: fretting-hand hammer-ons taken to their conclusion — both hands hammer notes on the fingerboard. Party trick until it's musical; the Unit 4 hammer-on drill is its foundation.
  • Natural harmonics: touch (don't press) directly over fret 12, 7, or 5 and pluck — bell tones. The 5th/7th-fret bells are also a tuning cross-check and the intro to "Portrait of Tracy".
  • Palm mute + thumb: not advanced at all, but paired with these it completes the tonal palette — thump, bell, clack, snap.

The fifth string (and beyond)

A 5-string adds a low B — five more semitones of floor (B0 ≈ 30.9 Hz) and, just as valuable, positional reach: the notes under your hand span more range without shifting. Everything on this site transfers: the B string is a fourth below E, so every shape, interval, and rule works unchanged (the theory library on this site already knows the tuning). Real adjustments: right-hand muting gets stricter (a floating thumb helps — the right-hand page flagged it), and taste matters more (the low B is seasoning; living below E1 turns mud). Try before buying; nothing in music requires it — plenty of pros never switch.

Harmonics: where the bells live

Over fret 12: the octave. Over 7: octave+fifth. Over 5: two octaves. Over 4 (and 9): two octaves + a third — faint but there. They're the string's overtone series made audible, which is also why the 5/7 harmonics can cross-check tuning: E@7-harmonic should beat-match A@5-harmonic.
