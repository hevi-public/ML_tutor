Harmonic analysis — reading the story

Given any chord chart, you can now name the pieces. Analysis is naming the plot: which chords are home, which are travel, which are visitors from other keys — so your line can tell the same story.

Before you start Diatonic families, the circle, and Roman-numeral fluency.

The method (four passes over any chart)

  1. Find the key — key signature, first/last chords, and where things feel resolved. Write the numerals over the letters.
  2. Mark the functions: home (I, vi), departure (ii, IV), tension (V, vii°). Every progression is trips between these three rooms.
  3. Flag the visitors — chords that don't fit the key (see below). They're the interesting bits.
  4. Find the bass story: strongest root motions (5ths falling), stepwise walks, pedal points. Your line should agree with the plot you just read.

The common "visitors" (non-diatonic chords with day jobs)

Worked example

  | C      | E7     | Am     | C7     |
    I        V7/vi    vi       V7/IV     ← the E7 and C7 are visitors:
  | F      | Fm     | C  G   | C      |    each one a dominant aimed
    IV       iv       I  V     I           at the NEXT chord
         borrowed! ↑

Listen for how E7 makes Am feel inevitable, and how Fm aches on the way home. That's function, audible.

The details (open as much as you like)

What analysis changes about your playing

Tension chords get leading approaches (that's what they're FOR); home chords get planted roots; secondary dominants beg for chromatic walks into their targets; a tritone sub means your bass gets a chromatic descent for free (D♭ falling to C). Analysis isn't homework — it's a list of invitations.

Modulation vs. tonicization

A visitor dominant that resolves and leaves = tonicization (the key never really changed). The band settling into the new key for a whole section = modulation (update your numerals!). The classic pop move — final chorus up a semitone or tone — is modulation at its most shameless, and the bass announces it.
