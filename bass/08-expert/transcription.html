Transcription — stealing like a musician

Lifting lines off records by ear is the single highest-value practice habit in existence: it trains ears, hands, vocabulary, and feel in one activity. Here's the method that makes it merely hard instead of impossible.

Before you start Some ear-trainer mileage — intervals at ~80% — and the fretboard map.

The method

  1. Choose winnable battles: clear mixes, moderate tempos, lines you already half-hum. (Motown > metal for audibility.)
  2. Loop tiny chunks — two bars at most. Any looping player works; slow-downers (browser extensions, most streaming apps' 0.75×) are legal performance-enhancers.
  3. Sing it first. If you can't sing the chunk, you haven't heard it yet — keep looping BEFORE touching the bass.
  4. Find the first note (hunt on the neck), then use interval ears for the rest: "up a fourth… down a step…".
  5. Get the rhythm honest: count it out loud against the record; ghost notes and rests are part of the line.
  6. Write it down — tab, notation, or your own scrawl. Writing is what moves it from "solved" to "owned".
  7. Play along until invisible: your line and the record become one sound. That's the rep that counts.

The details (open as much as you like)

A graded starter list

Level 1 (roots, clear): Bill Withers "Lean On Me", Neil Young "Harvest Moon". Level 2 (moving lines): "Stand By Me", "My Girl", "With or Without You". Level 3 (vocabulary): "What's Going On", "Come Together", "Money". Level 4 (grids & ghosts): "Sir Duke", "What Is Hip?", anything Jamerson that scares you. One song at your edge beats five beneath it.

When you're stuck on a note

Check the octave (bass fundamentals fool ears — try the same fret 12 higher); check for open strings (that resonance is a fingerprint); ask the harmony (it's probably a chord tone — which chord is passing?); and suspect ghost notes when the rhythm has more attacks than pitches. Still stuck? Move on and return — ears grow overnight, literally.

Tab sites vs. your ears

Checking a tab AFTER transcribing is studying; reading it INSTEAD is skipping the workout that was the entire point. (Also: the tab is often wrong, and now you can hear that it's wrong. Growth.)
