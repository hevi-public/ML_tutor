Improvisation & fills

Improvising isn't inventing from nothing — it's recombining vocabulary you own, inside a form you trust, with taste as the filter. You already have all three pieces; this page teaches the recombining.

Before you start Chord-tone fluency (Unit 5), the pentatonic boxes, and a few stolen lines in the notebook.

Fills: the gateway improvisation

A fill is a one-or-two-beat decoration at a phrase boundary. The rules of the craft:

  • Location: ends of 4- and 8-bar phrases, section seams, under held vocal notes — the built-in parking spots.
  • Aim: a fill is an approach note with ambition — it must LAND (usually on the next downbeat's root).
  • Material: pentatonic fragments, chord-tone runs, chromatic walks, one octave pop — the vocabulary you have.
  • Budget: one fill per phrase maximum. The bar you DON'T fill is what makes the one you do sound intentional.

The fill workshop

Three shapes into the same Am landing: scale walk, pentatonic tumble, chromatic slide. Steal, then vary.

Building a solo (yes, you)

Bass solos are rarer than guitar solos, and better for it. The method that works the first time: sing a phrase, play what you sang, breathe, repeat. The voice can't play anything your taste doesn't approve, so the filter is built in. Structure it like speech: statement, variation, contrast, home — over a form you know cold (the 12-bar is perfect).

The details (open as much as you like)

Motif thinking: one idea, four ways

Great improvisers develop, not enumerate. Take three notes (say R–♭3–4). Play them. Then: same rhythm higher; same notes different rhythm; backwards; leave a hole where a note was. One cell, one chorus of music — and listeners hear intent instead of scales.

Question & answer with yourself

Two-bar question (ends up in the air — off the root), two-bar answer (comes home). Loop the groove machine's vamp and hold this conversation for ten minutes. It's the fastest route to solos with shape — and secretly it's also how the best FILLS relate to the line around them.
