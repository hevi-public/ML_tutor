Chord extensions & alterations

C13♯11 looks like a password. It's actually a polite stack of thirds with two notes tweaked — and the bassist's response to almost all of it is beautifully simple.

Before you start Seventh chords and their symbols, cold.

The idea

Keep stacking thirds past the 7th and you get the extensions: the 9th (a 2nd an octave up), 11th (a 4th up high), 13th (a 6th up high). They add color, not function — a C13 still behaves like a C7 that dressed up. Alterations bend extensions or the 5th by a semitone (♭9, ♯9, ♯11, ♭13, ♭5, ♯5) — mostly on dominant chords, to sharpen their pull.

The liberating rule Numbers above 7 are the pianist's and guitarist's business. Your root (and 3rd/♭7 when you choose) supports every one of them. Never fight the upstairs — a low 9th or 11th under the band turns color into mud. Big chords ask the bass for MORE simplicity, not less.

The details (open as much as you like)

Why the 11th is the troublemaker

Over a major 3rd, the natural 11 sits a semitone above it — the one rub that reads as error rather than color. Hence ♯11 on major chords (the Lydia­n note) and plain 11 only on minor chords (where the ♭3 leaves room). If you ever wondered why "sus" chords remove the 3rd: it's to make room for exactly this note.

When the bass DOES play extensions

High-register fills, chordal moments (solo bass, duos), and melodic hooks — deliberately, upstairs, where they belong. The 9th two octaves above the root is gorgeous; the same note a 2nd above your root is a cluster. Register is the whole game.
