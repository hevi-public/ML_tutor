Next steps — the map for the years after

This site can take you from "which one is the E string?" to walking a jazz blues. What it can't do is be a band. Here's the honest map of what comes next, and how everything you built here keeps compounding.

The one non-negotiable: play with people

Every skill on this site exists for the moment another human counts to four. Nothing — not one lab, not one exercise — substitutes for it, and nothing accelerates you faster. Bassists have a structural advantage: everyone needs one. Realistic entry points:

  • Jams and open mics — blues jams especially: the form is the 12-bar you own, and "blues in A?" is a complete rehearsal.
  • One other person — a guitarist or pianist friend and a chart. Duos expose everything kindly.
  • Community ensembles, church bands, school groups — structured, forgiving, regular. Regular beats impressive.
  • Online collaboration — trading stems counts; timing against someone else's take is real ensemble skill.

Your weekly diet (the maintenance version)

  • Daily-ish: the routine builder keeps technique and repertoire honest in 20 minutes.
  • Weekly: one steal; a few minutes of ear trainer; one groove-machine session in a style you're worst at.
  • Monthly: record yourself playing a full song and listen like a producer. The recording is still the honest mirror.

Where to go deeper (a short, opinionated list)

  • Reading: Standing in the Shadows of Motown (Jamerson transcriptions — a bible), Ed Friedland's Building Walking Bass Lines, Rufus Reid's The Evolving Bassist (upright perspective that improves electric players).
  • Listening as study: pick one bassist per month and binge them: Jamerson → Jaco → Duck Dunn → Carol Kaye → Paul McCartney → Bootsy → Pino Palladino → Esperanza Spalding. Steal one thing from each.
  • A teacher, occasionally: even two or three lessons a year from a good teacher catches habits no website can see. Bring them a recording of yourself; that's where lessons start mattering.

What "expert" actually meant

You now read notation and charts, know the neck as geography, hear intervals and chord qualities, build lines in six styles, and — the real one — know how to practice. That last skill is the compounding asset: everything else on this list is just choosing where to point it. The site's reference pages (glossary, symbols, search, flashcards) stay useful forever; the concept map should be pleasingly green by now.

The last exercise on the site Go be a bass player. Find three humans and a garage. Everything else is details.
