Soul & Motown — the singable bass line

In 1960s Detroit, James Jamerson turned the bass part into a second melody — syncopated, chromatic, joyful — without ever dropping the floor. This page is that vocabulary: lines you could hum.

Before you start The approach-note grammar and the dotted-quarter + eighth rhythm — soul runs on both.

The vocabulary

  • The pulse: the dotted-quarter + eighth ("1 … &-of-2") and its variants — momentum without sixteenth-note anxiety.
  • Chromatic connections everywhere: Jamerson walks between chord tones through every crack in the fretboard — approach notes as a lifestyle.
  • Open strings as springboards — quick open-string eighths between fretted notes keep lines flowing (and famously let one hand do impossible things).
  • Melody, but never at the floor's expense: roots still own the changes; the singing happens between them.

The groove

Play along: groove machine — Motown pulse, I–vi–IV–V in G.

The details (open as much as you like)

The Jamerson recipe, reverse-engineered

Take the plain root-fifth skeleton. Now: replace straight repeats with the dotted pulse; connect every change chromatically; drop an open-string eighth before big jumps; add ONE ghosted sixteenth per phrase; end phrases on the third for sweetness. That's a caricature of genius — but it's a playable one, and the exercises drill each move.

Muted thump: the era's tone

Sixties records: flatwound strings, foam under the bridge, plucked with fingers or thumb — a warm, short thump. Get 80% of it today with the palm mute and the tone knob rolled halfway down. Note length does the rest.

Listening list

The Temptations "My Girl" (the walk-up as hook), Stevie Wonder "For Once in My Life" (Jamerson unleashed), Marvin Gaye "What's Going On" (the ballad masterpiece), Aretha Franklin "Respect" (Tommy Cogbill), Al Green "Love and Happiness" (Memphis restraint). Hum the bass lines — you'll find you already can.
