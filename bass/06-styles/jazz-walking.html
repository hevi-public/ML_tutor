Unit 6 · Styles

Jazz walking — the conversation

Unit 5 taught the walking formula. Jazz is where the formula becomes speech: swung, connected, endlessly varied, in dialogue with the ride cymbal. This page upgrades the recipe into the style.

Before you start Walking 101 cold, and the swing feel in your body.

What changes from Walking 101

  • The feel: quarters swing — fat, connected (legato), each note leaning into the next. The classic instruction: "play the quarter notes long".
  • The recipe loosens: beat 1 can now be a third or fifth if the line demands it (the ear you've built decides).
  • Enclosures join the vocabulary: surround the target from both sides (above, below, land) — the chromatic approach's fancy sibling.
  • Skips for drama: an octave leap or a big drop resets the line's register — walking isn't only stepwise.
  • The drummer is your duet partner: your quarters and the ride cymbal's "ding ding-a-ding" are one instrument.

The line

Bars: Cmaj7 · Am7 · Dm7 · G7 — the "rhythm changes" turnaround. Spot the enclosure into bar 3. Play along: groove machine — walking ii–V–I in C.

The details (open as much as you like)

Long quarters: the sound of jazz bass

Classical players separate; jazz walkers connect. Each quarter rings right up to the next attack — on upright it's the natural sound, on bass guitar you create it: fretting hand holds, plucking alternation smooth, no gaps. Record two bars staccato then legato: the second one is jazz.

Skip-a-beat variations (the drop and the push)

Once steady quarters are effortless, jazz decorates them: the occasional two eighth-notes on beat 4 (a little run into the next bar), a triplet drag, or a dead-note skip. Rule of taste: one decoration per four bars, and never at the expense of the quarter pulse. The pulse IS the gig.

Listening list

Ray Brown with Oscar Peterson ("Night Train" — the definition), Paul Chambers on "Kind of Blue" (walking as architecture), Ron Carter with Miles ("So What" live versions), Christian McBride (the modern standard). Follow the bass for one full song; notice you always know where the harmony is.
