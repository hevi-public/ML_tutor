Unit 6 · Styles

Rock — the straight-eighth engine

Driving, even eighth notes on the root, welded to the kick drum. It sounds like the simplest thing on this site — and playing it great for three minutes is a genuine skill.

Before you start Even eighths with strict alternation and the accent control from timing & groove.

The recipe

  • Eighth-note roots, relentless and even — the engine room of AC/DC, Tom Petty, punk, and most of the radio.
  • Note length is the sound: connected-but-separate, each note handed to the next. All-legato turns to mush; too staccato turns nervous.
  • Dynamics sit at a strong medium, with accents (beat 1, or wherever the kick lands) not extra speed.
  • Changes announced on beat 1, sometimes approached with a quick scale walk in the last beat.

The groove

Then play along with the band: groove machine — rock 8ths, I–V–vi–IV in C.

The details (open as much as you like)

Pick vs fingers (the honest answer)

Plenty of rock is played with a pick: brighter attack, natural evenness at speed, and the downstroke chug is its own sound. Fingers get you 95% of the way with more tonal range. If you're curious, learn downstrokes-only first (the punk engine), then alternate picking. It's an accent, not an identity.

Variations that keep eighths interesting

The craft is micro-variation: drop to the fifth for the bar's second half; lift to the octave for a chorus; a quick 3-note scale walk into the next chord (the approach-note page, faster); one strategic push (hitting the change an eighth early, with the kick). One variation per section — rock rewards restraint with size.

Listening list (find these bass lines)

AC/DC "Back in Black" (the discipline), Tom Petty "American Girl" (eighths with lift), Ramones — any track (downstroke commitment), Foo Fighters "Everlong" (eighths as architecture), Queen "Another One Bites the Dust" (when rock borrows funk). Listen for note LENGTH as much as pitch.
