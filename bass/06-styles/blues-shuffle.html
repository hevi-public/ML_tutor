Blues shuffle — the long-short skip

Take even eighths and lean them: LOOONG-short, LOOONG-short. That lean is swing, the shuffle is its strut, and the 12-bar form from Unit 5 is its home address.

Before you start The 12-bar form and its boogie line — this page changes the FEEL of what you already play.

The feel, explained honestly

Under a shuffle, each beat divides into a triplet ("1-trip-let"): you play the first and third slices and skip the middle — long-short, roughly 2:1. Nobody counts that fraction while playing; you install it by feel. Say "HUM-pty DUM-pty" in rhythm — that's a shuffle. The demos here play it; your job is to lock to it until straight eighths feel like a choice, not a default.

The groove

Written as triplet-feel eighths — the notation shows pairs; the LEAN is applied by the player (and by this site's playback here). Play along: groove machine — shuffle blues in A.

Shuffle craft

  • The boogie line shuffled (R-3-5-6 in pairs) is the canonical part — bar for bar through the whole 12-bar form.
  • Two-feel option: for verses, drop to roots on 1 and 3 (like a slow heartbeat), then kick into the full shuffle for solos — instant arrangement.
  • The short note is quiet. The skip reads as feel when the "short" is lighter; equal volume turns the strut into a limp.
  • Slow blues = 12/8: the same triplet DNA at ballad tempo, all six slices felt, the mightiest groove in the idiom.

The details (open as much as you like)

How swung is swung?

The 2:1 triplet split is the schoolbook answer; real players slide between nearly-straight (fast tempos flatten the swing) and harder than 2:1 (slow, greasy shuffles). The band agrees by ear in the first bar. Your job is matching the drummer's ride cymbal, not a fraction.

Listening list

Muddy Waters "Hoochie Coochie Man" (stop-time + shuffle), Stevie Ray Vaughan "Pride and Joy" (Tommy Shannon's masterclass), T-Bone Walker "Stormy Monday" (slow-blues 12/8), BB King "Every Day I Have the Blues" (big-band shuffle). Count the triplets out loud against any of them.
