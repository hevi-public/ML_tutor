Reggae — the groove of what you don't play

Reggae bass is huge, melodic, and mostly silence. The radical move: beat one is often empty — the whole feel hangs from the spaces. If funk taught precision, reggae teaches trust.

Before you start Note-length control and rests as actions (rhythm unit).

The anatomy of the one drop

  • "One drop" = the drums drop beat 1 (kick + rim land on beat 3). The bass often rests on 1 too — the bar starts with air.
  • The bass phrase is a melody: short, fat, repeated exactly, usually from root–fifth–octave and the minor pentatonic.
  • Fat and short: palm-muted or fleshy-thumbed notes, deep tone (neck-position pluck, tone rolled off), endings placed as carefully as attacks.
  • Repetition is sacred. The same two-bar phrase for five minutes IS the arrangement — variation is a special event.

The groove

Feel the empty ONE. Play along: groove machine — one drop on A (pads play the off-beat skank).

The details (open as much as you like)

Surviving the empty beat 1

Everything you've trained says "play the one" — reggae says feel it and stay silent. The trick: keep counting out loud, treat the silence as a note you're playing (it has an exact start!), and anchor to the guitar's off-beat "skank" on 2 and 4. When the empty One stops feeling like falling, you've got it.

Variants: steppers & rockers

Not all reggae drops the one: "steppers" puts the kick on every beat (four on the floor); "rockers" adds syncopated punch. The bass principles — melodic phrase, fat-short notes, sacred repetition — hold across all of them.

Listening list

Bob Marley "Natural Mystic" & "Stir It Up" (Aston "Family Man" Barrett — the vocabulary), Toots & the Maytals "Pressure Drop", Gregory Isaacs "Night Nurse" (Flabba Holt), Black Uhuru "Guess Who's Coming to Dinner" (Robbie Shakespeare). Listen for where notes END.
