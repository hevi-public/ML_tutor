Unit 1 · Reading Music

Rhythm & counting

Wrong note, right time: the band barely flinches. Right note, wrong time: everyone hears it. For a bassist, rhythm literacy IS literacy.

Before you start Nothing but a steady foot. Notes' names were last page; this page is entirely about when.

The idea

Time in music is a grid. The beat is the steady pulse; a bar boxes up (usually) four of them. Written rhythm just says how many boxes each note fills: a ♩ quarter note fills one beat, a ♪ eighth note half a beat, a half note two, a whole note four. Silence is written too — rests fill boxes with deliberate quiet.

And the trade secret: musicians don't feel rhythm as math, they feel it as syllables. Quarters: "1, 2, 3, 4". Eighths: "1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &" (say "and" for &). Count out loud — it feels silly for exactly two days, and then you can read rhythm.

Hear the grid

Toggle boxes on the eighth-note grid, press play, and count along with the clicks — the accented click is beat 1.

The grid is one bar of 4/4 sliced into eighths: cells read "1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &". Count out loud while it plays — the counting is the skill.

    The details (open as much as you like)

    Dots and ties — the length modifiers

    A dot after a note adds half its own length: dotted quarter = 1½ beats ("1 & 2", landing again on "& of 2"). A tie gluing two notes means pluck once, hold through both — it's how a note reaches across a bar line. Dotted-quarter + eighth is the rhythm of thousands of bass lines; you'll drill it below.

    Rests are a bass superpower

    Reggae, funk, and Motown lines are famous for the holes in them. A rest means stop the string — with the fretting hand relaxing or the plucking hand touching down (muting). When you practice the exercises below, treat every rest as an action with a precise landing time, not a gap where you wait.

    Why count out loud, really?

    Because it forces the subdivision to run continuously in your head, and steady subdivision is what "great time" physically is. Silent counting collapses under pressure; spoken counting survives it. Every rhythm you can say precisely, you can play precisely — it's remarkably reliable in both directions.

    Practice

    All on one note (open A) on purpose — zero fretboard distraction, one hundred percent time. Count out loud on every rep.
