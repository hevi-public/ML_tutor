Unit 1 · Reading Music

The staff & bass clef

Written music is a graph: time runs left to right, pitch runs bottom to top. Learn five lines and one fancy F, and the graph starts talking.

Before you start Just the note-name alphabet (A to G, then repeat) — covered in Unit 0. You do not need to read music already; that's what this unit is for.

The idea

The notes live on a grid of five lines called the staff. Higher on the grid = higher pitch; each line and each space between lines is one letter name. But five anonymous lines could start anywhere — so music plants a flag: the 𝄢 bass clef wraps its two dots around the second line from the top, declaring this line is F. From that one anchor, every other line and space follows alphabetically.

In plain English The clef is the "you are here" marker. Bass clef: fourth line up = F. Count lines and spaces up or down the alphabet from there.

Where your strings live

Notes that spill off the grid get mini extension lines — ledger lines… no wait, those are called ledger lines (tab comes two pages from now). Your open E string sits on the first ledger line below the staff; the other three open strings climb from there:

One honest small print: bass parts are written an octave higher than they sound — otherwise almost everything would drown in ledger lines below the staff. Every bass player on Earth reads this way; you never have to think about it again.

The details (open as much as you like)

How to actually learn the notes (not just the mnemonic)

Mnemonics bootstrap; landmarks make you fluent. Burn in three anchors: F on the clef's own line, A at the top line, and middle-of-the-staff D. Read neighbors as "one above A", "the space under D" — exactly like you learned the fretboard by landmarks, and much faster than counting from the bottom every time.

Why a "bass" clef at all?

Clefs slide the grid to fit an instrument's range. The treble clef (𝄞) anchors G above middle C for high instruments; the bass clef anchors F below it. Same system, different windows. You only need bass clef — but knowing why guitar tab sites look "wrong" (they're in treble) saves confusion.

Practice

Reading and playing must fuse into one act — every exercise here is "see it, say it, play it".
