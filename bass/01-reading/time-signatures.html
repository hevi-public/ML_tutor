Time signatures

The two stacked numbers are a promise about how time is boxed. Most music you'll play keeps one promise — 4/4 — but the others feel wonderful in the body, and bassists are the ones who make them feel right.

Before you start Counting quarters and eighths out loud (rhythm & counting).

The idea

A time signature like 4/4 says: top number — how many beats per bar; bottom number — which note value counts as one beat (4 = quarter note, 8 = eighth note). So 4/4 is four quarter-note beats per bar; 3/4 is three.

But the signature also implies a stress pattern: 4/4 leans ONE-two-THREE-four (with 1 the strongest); 3/4 spins ONE-two-three; 6/8 rolls in two big pulses, each made of three little ones — ONE-two-three FOUR-five-six. Same clock speed, totally different dance.

The details (open as much as you like)

6/8 vs 3/4 — the classic confusion, settled

Both contain six eighth notes' worth of time, but they group differently: 3/4 is three beats subdivided in twos (ONE-and TWO-and THREE-and); 6/8 is two beats subdivided in threes (ONE-two-three FOUR-five-six). Your foot taps 3 times per bar in 3/4 and 2 times per bar in 6/8. When in doubt, find the foot.

What the bassist does about all this

The bass states the meter more than any other instrument: landing the root on beat 1 is what tells everyone's body where the bar starts. In 3/4 the classic move is root on 1, lighter notes (or nothing) on 2–3; in 6/8, roots on the two big pulses. The practice exercises below build exactly that instinct.

Practice

Root-on-the-strong-beat drills in three meters. The notes are trivial on purpose — your job is to make beat 1 feel inevitable.
