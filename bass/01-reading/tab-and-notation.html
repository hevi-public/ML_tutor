Tab ↔ notation

Tab tells your fingers where. Notation tells your ears what and when. This site shows both, stacked — here's how to read the pair as one thing.

Before you start The staff (previous-but-one page) and counting (previous page).

The idea

Tab is a picture of your bass: four lines = four strings, with the low E string at the bottom — exactly like the fretboard diagrams you've been tapping. A number on a line means "press that fret on that string"; 0 means open. It's instantly readable… and it's silent about rhythm. Plain tab can't say whether a note lasts two beats or half of one.

That's why this site (like most good bass books) stacks tab under real notation: read the rhythm from the top staff, take the fingering from the bottom one. Same music, two lenses.

Same phrase, both lenses

The details (open as much as you like)

Why the same note appears at different frets

Remember from the fretboard unit (or discover now): most pitches live in several places — the D at A-string fret 5 is also D-string fret 0. Notation leaves the choice to you; tab records one specific choice. When this site's tab picks a position, it's optimizing for staying in one hand position — feel free to disagree once you know why.

Internet tab, honestly

Text tab from the internet is wonderful and wrong: fingerings are often awkward, rhythms absent, and errors common. Use it as a sketch — then let the recording be the authority. (That's also why the transcription skills in the Expert unit beat any tab site: your ears don't have typos.)
