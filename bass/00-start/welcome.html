Welcome — how to use this site

This is a bass course that runs entirely in your browser: fretboards you can tap, notation that plays itself, a metronome, ear training, and a path that goes from "which one is the E string?" to reading real charts.

What this site believes

Three things, and everything else follows from them:

1. Plain words first. Every idea starts in everyday language, and the official term arrives only after you already understand the thing it names. When we say "notes that are two frets apart", you'll learn later that musicians call that a whole step — and by then it's just a new name for something you already know. Any term with a dotted underline is tappable and will explain itself.

2. Your ears and hands come along. Music theory that you can't hear is trivia. Every example on this site is playable — tap a fret diagram and it sounds, press ▶ under a line of notation and it plays. And every page ends with something concrete to practice on a real bass, with a starting tempo and a target.

3. Small, honest steps. Each page teaches one idea, checks it with a short quiz (asked in plain words, not vocabulary), and tells you where it leads. Quiz questions you miss turn into flashcards automatically, so the things you personally find hard come back around.

How a page works

Every topic page has the same flow:

  • The idea — a plain-English explanation, usually with an everyday analogy.
  • Hear it / find it — an interactive diagram or playable notation. Sound always starts with your tap — nothing plays on its own.
  • The details — collapsible layers. Open as much as you like; you can follow the whole course without ever opening the deepest layer.
  • Practice — exercises with notation and tab, a ▶ button so you know what they should sound like, tempo targets, and a "log practice" button that remembers what you've worked on.
  • A quiz — a few questions to let you claim the page. Finishing the quiz marks the page complete on the concept map; it never blocks you from moving on.
Handy keys ← and → move between pages. Press / anywhere to search — plain phrasing works ("two frets up", "the fancy F symbol").

What you need

  • A bass. Any 4-string bass works — this course uses standard tuning (E–A–D–G) throughout. Five-string players: everything here applies; there's an extended-range page in the Expert unit.
  • Something to hear it through — an amp, headphones via an amp/interface, or even unplugged practice for the early units.
  • 15–30 minutes at a time. Short, regular sessions with a clear goal beat long, occasional ones. The practice log and routine builder are built around that idea.

Where to start

If you're brand new: just follow the arrows — the next page covers your instrument and getting set up, then tuning, then reading music.

If you already play a bit: take the self-assessment at the end of this unit. It asks a handful of honest questions ("can you name the note at the 5th fret of the A string without counting?") and suggests where to jump in. The concept map shows the whole path at a glance, and nothing stops you from wandering.
