Gear & setup

Ten minutes of naming parts and adjusting straps saves months of fighting your own instrument. None of this requires spending money.

The parts that matter

Thickest string (E) at the top of this view — when the bass hangs on you, that string is closest to the ceiling.

  • Tuning machines — the pegs you'll meet on the next page. Tighter = higher.
  • Nut and frets — the string vibrates between the bridge and either the nut (open) or the fret you press.
  • Pickups — magnetic microphones under the strings. Pluck over different pickups and listen: near the neck = round and deep, near the bridge = tight and growly.
  • Volume & tone knobs — day-one settings: volume up, tone around the middle. That's genuinely all you need for now.

Holding the bass (this is technique, not comfort trivia)

  • Strap height: adjust so the bass sits in the same place standing or sitting — bridge roughly at your belt, neck angled slightly up (30–40°). Too low looks cool and wrecks your fretting wrist; too high cramps your plucking arm.
  • Sitting: body of the bass on your right thigh (if right-handed), and still use the strap — the left hand should never be holding the neck up, only playing it.
  • Fretting hand: thumb pad on the back of the neck, roughly opposite your middle finger, like holding a peach you don't want to bruise. Fingers curl and press just behind the fret, not on top of it, with the fingertip.
  • Plucking hand: rest your thumb on a pickup, and pluck with the pads of your index and middle fingers — alternating. (A pick is legitimate too; this course defaults to fingers.)
  • Both shoulders down. Check every few minutes at first; tension you practice becomes tension you perform.
The honest gear list A working bass, a cable, anything that makes it audible (amp, headphone amp, or audio interface), and ideally a strap and a tuner (this site has one). Nothing else. New strings and a professional setup (action + intonation) are the two cheap upgrades that genuinely help a struggling instrument.

The details (open as much as you like)

Buying advice, kept short

Any big-brand entry bass (Squier, Yamaha, Ibanez, Harley Benton…) is more instrument than a beginner can outgrow in years. Prefer a 4-string, standard scale. Secondhand is great — bring a player, or check: straight neck, no fret buzz everywhere, working electronics, tuners that hold. Budget for a setup before budgeting for a better bass.

What a "setup" actually adjusts

A tech will set the neck relief (truss rod), string height (action), and intonation (so fretted notes are in tune up the neck), and check the nut. It transforms cheap basses. If notes buzz everywhere or the 12th fret is audibly out of tune with the open string, that's setup territory — not your fault.
