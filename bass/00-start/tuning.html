Tuning up

An out-of-tune bass makes everything you play sound wrong — including the things you play correctly. Thirty seconds of tuning buys you an hour of honest feedback.

The idea

Your four strings are named for the notes they play when open: E, A, D, G, from the thickest to the thinnest. Tuning means turning each string's machine head (the pegs on the headstock) until the string's pitch matches its reference note.

Turning the peg tightens or loosens the string: tighter = higher, looser = lower. Small turns — an out-of-tune string is usually only a hair away.

Hear your references

Tap a pad, then play your matching open string and adjust until the two sounds melt into one.

With "hold the tone" on, the reference sustains while you tune — listen for the slow wobble (beating) between the two sounds; it gets slower as you get closer, and vanishes when you're in tune.

How to match a pitch by ear

  • Play the reference and your string at the same time.
  • If you can't tell higher from lower: tune your string clearly down until it's obviously too low, then come up slowly.
  • Near the target you'll hear a slow wah-wah-wah wobble — that's the two pitches interfering. Slower wobble = closer. No wobble = in tune.
  • Always finish by tuning up to the note — it keeps the string from slipping flat while you play.

The details (open as much as you like)

The 5th-fret cross-check

Once one string is right, the bass can check itself: the 5th fret of each string sounds the same note as the next open string. Hold E-string fret 5 (that's an A) and compare it to your open A string; then A-string fret 5 against open D; then D-string fret 5 against open G. The second practice exercise below walks it.

Why basses drift out of tune

Strings are steel under a lot of tension: temperature swings, fresh strings still stretching, enthusiastic playing, and bumping the pegs all shift them. Checking takes seconds — make it the first thing you do every time you pick the bass up, and it becomes automatic ear training for free.

Tuners, clip-ons, and this site

Ear-matching is a skill worth having, but there's no shame in a tuner — clip-on tuners read the vibration through the headstock and work in loud rooms. This site's Practice Labs unit includes a microphone tuner that listens to your bass and shows you exactly how far off each string is.
