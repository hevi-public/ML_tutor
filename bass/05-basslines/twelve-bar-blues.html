Unit 5 · Bass Lines

The 12-bar blues — everyone's common language

Walk into any jam on the planet, say "blues in A", count four — and total strangers play a song together. This is the form that makes that possible. Learn it until it's not memory but geography.

Before you start I, IV, V and dominant 7ths. Blues bends one rule: ALL three chords are played as dominant 7s.

The map

I7I7I7I7
IV7IV7I7I7
V7IV7I7V7 (turnaround)

Three four-bar sentences: make yourself at home (4 bars) — go visit the IV (2) and come back (2) — build the tension (V–IV) and resolve, with the last bar (turnaround) launching the next chorus. In A: A7 / D7 / E7. In G: G7 / C7 / D7. The Roman numerals are the form; keys are just wardrobe.

Hear the form

Follow the highlight and say the numerals out loud: "one… one… one… one… FOUR, four, one, one, FIVE, four, one, five!"

The quick change (and other dialects)

Common variants you'll meet: quick change — bar 2 goes to IV7 (…and bar 1 stays I7); slow blues in 12/8 — same map, rolling triplet feel; minor blues — i7, iv7, V7 (only the V stays dominant); 8-bar blues — a compressed cousin. Learn the standard form cold and the dialects are one-sentence updates.

Why dominant 7ths everywhere doesn't break theory

By diatonic rules only V gets a dominant 7 — blues ignores this and puts that restless ♭7 buzz on every chord. That's not sloppy harmony; it IS the blues sound: three chords that all lean, forever unresolved. Theory describes; blues predates the description.

Turnaround vocabulary, starter kit

Bar 12's job is re-launch. Three reliable moves: sit on V7; walk up to it (I–ii–iii–V feel: A–B–C♯–E); or the chromatic slide down from I's octave into V. Fills live here too — but a turnaround that just lands beat 1 of the next chorus solidly beats a flashy one that doesn't.
