Roots & chord tones — the 80% of everything

Root, fifth, octave, then the third and seventh: rank a chord's notes by job security and you've ranked most bass lines ever recorded. Here's the hierarchy and how to spend it.

The two moves to steal today

Root–fifth alternation — the two-beat heartbeat under country, polka, and half of rock. Remember the low fifth trick: same fret, one string down.

Root–octave bounce — the energy machine of disco and funk (finger 1 and finger 4, the fixed claw from Unit 2).

The details (open as much as you like)

Strong beats want strong notes

The working rule of nearly all bass playing: chord tones on the beats, anything you like between them. Beats 1 and 3 carry the harmony; land root/3rd/5th/7th there and the line sounds "inside" no matter what happens on the &s. This one rule generates everything from Motown to walking jazz.

Thirds: the polite warning

The third is gorgeous mid-bar and risky on the bottom of beat 1 (it makes the chord sound inverted — sometimes wanted, often not). Standard craft: root on the change, third later in the bar. When the chart WANTS the third under the chord it will say so with a slash (G/B). Exceptions are for when you can name them.
