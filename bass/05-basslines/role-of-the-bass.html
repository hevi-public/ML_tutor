Unit 5 · Bass Lines

The bassist's job

The bass sits exactly where rhythm meets harmony — one hand in the drummer's world, one in the chords. Understand the job description and every note-choice question gets easier.

Before you start Diatonic harmony and a steady pocket.

The two duties

Duty 1 — say when. With the kick drum, you define where the beat lives. People's bodies move to the bass; if you rush, the whole room rushes.

Duty 2 — say what. When the chord changes, your note is what tells everyone's ear the harmony moved. Play the root on the change and the band sounds organized. This is why the same guitar chord over a different bass note is a different chord to the listener — you hold the harmony's steering wheel (remember slash chords: C/E is an instruction to you).

The whole unit in one sentence Land the right note (usually the root) at the right moment (usually the chord change), keep the pocket, and decorate only in the space that's left.

Space is a note choice

Listen to any beloved bass line: the holes are composed as carefully as the notes. Rests create the room that makes the next note land; constant sound is wallpaper. The discipline of Unit 4's note-length work becomes, here, an aesthetic: you are allowed to be simple, and the greats usually are.

The details (open as much as you like)

Locking with the kick drum, concretely

"Lock with the drummer" means: know where the kick pattern is, land your low notes with it (or in deliberate dialogue with it), and match note endings to the drums' space. In the groove machine lab you can hear root-cue patterns against the click — practice matching them exactly, then leaving gaps where they leave gaps.

When NOT to play the root

Slash chords (play what's written), walking lines (roots on ones, travel elsewhere), pedal points (hold one note while chords move — instant drama), and melodic hooks where the line IS the song. All are deliberate exceptions that prove the rule: everyone can hear when the bass leaves home, which is exactly why it works.
