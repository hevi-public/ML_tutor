Unit 5 · Bass Lines

Approach notes — aiming at the next chord

The difference between chords-played-in-a-row and a line is one small habit: spending the last moment of each bar traveling toward the next root. Three ways to travel, one rule about timing.

Before you start Chord tones on the beats — this page is about the space between the beats.

The idea

An approach note is played just before a target (usually the next chord's root), one step away from it, so the landing sounds aimed rather than teleported. The three flavors:

  • Chromatic — one fret above or below the target. Works over any chords, in any key, because it borrows its meaning entirely from where it's going. The pro's pocketknife.
  • Scale (diatonic) — one scale step above or below. Smoother, more "inside" than chromatic.
  • Dominant — the fifth of the target, dropped in just before it. The strongest pull in harmony, miniaturized.
The timing rule Approach notes live on weak moments — the last eighth or last beat of the bar. Target lands ON the change. Tension on the weak, resolution on the strong: that's the whole grammar.

Hear the difference

Same G–C–D–G progression; only the last eighth note of each bar changes. Small habit, different instrument.

The details (open as much as you like)

Why chromatic approaches never sound wrong

A note a semitone from its target resolves so fast the ear files it as motion, not harmony — it's heard as belonging to where it's GOING. That's why F♯ works before G even in keys with no F♯: context is destination. The only way to break a chromatic approach is to be late — then it's just a wrong note standing still.

Passing tones: the same idea, mid-bar

A passing tone connects two chord tones by step on a weak beat (root … 2 … 3rd). Same grammar as approaches — non-chord notes are fine while moving on weak time. Put one on a strong beat and hold it, and you'll hear why the rule exists.

Double chromatic & enclosure (preview of fancier moves)

Two frets away, walking in by semitones (F–F♯–G), or surrounding the target from both sides (A♭–F♯–G — an "enclosure"): jazz vocabulary built from the same brick. Meet them properly in walking bass and the Expert unit.
