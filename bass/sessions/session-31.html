Session 31 · Phase 6 — Expert moves

Extensions: 9s, 11s, 13s

"C13♯11" looks like a Wi-Fi password. It's actually just the seventh-chord stack from Session 16 kept climbing — and the bassist's question is never "can I play all that" but "which one note adds the shimmer".

Tune up

Warm-up: the four flavors

Amaj7 · A7 · Am7 · Am7♭5 arpeggios — the stacks you're about to extend.

New thing: climb to the ninth

Keep stacking thirds past the 7th and the next rung is the 9th — the same note as the 2nd, an octave up, and the prettiest cheap thrill on the instrument. Climb A7 and Am7 all the way up and let the top note hang.

Why that worked

Extensions are the stack continuing: 9, 11, 13 — which are just degrees 2, 4 and 6 wearing octave heels. The symbol grammar: the biggest number includes the floors below (C13 implies the 7 and 9), alterations in parentheses or superscript (♯11, ♭9) adjust single rungs. And the bassist's golden rule: you already play the foundation — root, 5, ♭7 — so extensions are seasoning notes for fills and passing moments, one at a time, not chords to spell.

The 11 is a troublemaker

Over a chord with a major 3rd, the natural 11 (=4) sits a half-step above it and clangs — which is why charts write ♯11 (the floaty Lydian note) on major chords, and plain 11 mostly appears on minor chords where the ♭3 leaves it room. If you remember one hazard sign from this session, this is it. Full grammar with played stacks: chord extensions.

Apply it: season a vamp with the 9

Groove on Am7, foundation first — then let fills touch the 9 (B) at phrase ends and feel the line turn expensive. One seasoning note per phrase; the foundation never leaves.

Band: A vamp, Motown pulse, 84 — treat it as Am7 and keep the fills minor-flavored.

Quick check

Wrap up

Passwords decoded: tall symbols are stacked thirds, the big number includes the floors, and one well-hung 9 outclasses a spelled-out 13. Seasoning, not spelling — that's expert restraint.
