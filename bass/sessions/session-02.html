New thing: the two-finger engine

Anchor your thumb on the pickup. Now pluck the open E with your index finger, then your middle finger, then index, then middle — like walking, finger after finger. Each fingertip pulls across the string and comes to rest against your thumb-side… and that's the whole technique.

Tap ♩ 70 in the bar below and make every pluck land exactly on a click.

Why that worked

Two fingers taking strict turns is called alternation, and it's the engine behind almost everything a fingerstyle bassist plays: one finger doing all the work hits a speed ceiling in days; two never do. Plucking across the string (a rest stroke, landing on the next string) is what makes the note thick instead of twangy.

And the number on the metronome? Tempo, in beats per minute — 70 means seventy clicks each minute, and your job is to sit so exactly on them that the click seems to disappear into your note. Players call that swallowing the click.

Which finger starts? And where should the thumb go later?

Nobody checks which finger starts — pick whichever, then alternate strictly even when a pattern would be easier repeating one finger; that's what builds the reflex. The thumb anchored on the pickup is the day-one home; later it learns to float onto the E string to quiet it while you play higher strings. That trick — and where exactly to pluck for fat vs. punchy — lives in the right-hand page below.

Apply it: cross the strings

Same engine, now touring all four strings — the alternation must not reset when you change strings. If index ended the E string, middle starts the A string.

Feeling brave? Double up: two plucks per click, still strictly alternating. You just invented eighth notes — we'll name them properly in Session 5.
