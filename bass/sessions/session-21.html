Walking bass 101

Four quarter notes a bar, never stopping, each bar a little road from this chord to the next. Walking sounds like jazz wizardry; it's actually a formula — and you already own every ingredient.

Warm-up: announcements

The E–A vamp with chromatic approaches on beat 4 — walking's seed.

New thing: the four-beat formula

Each bar: beat 1 — root. Beat 2 — a chord tone. Beat 3 — a scale note. Beat 4 — an approach into the next root. That's walking. Here's a full written chorus of the jazz handshake (Dm7 · G7 · Cmaj7) to steal, formula labeled under every note.

Why that worked

Walking is the whole course in four beats: strong beats get strong notes (root on 1, chord tone on 2), lighter beats get connective tissue (scale on 3), and beat 4 does Session 20's job — announcing the next arrival. The commitment is rhythmic: quarter notes never stop. No gaps, no doubling up; the unbroken chain IS the style.

Notice you made choices: which chord tone, which scale note, up or down. Different choices, different road, same formula — that's why two bassists never walk the same line and both are right.

Making it swing (a preview)

Real jazz walking leans its quarters with a triplet undertow and occasionally cracks a beat into a skip. That's feel, and it arrives in Session 29 with the style itself. Today is about the note-choosing machine; the classic bar-shapes and the ii–V squeeze live in walking bass 101.
