Analyze a song in four passes

Analysis sounds like homework; it's actually reconnaissance. Four quick passes over any chart tell your hands what to play before the count-in — and today you run the method on a song with a spy in it.

Tune up

Warm-up: the 9 hang

A9 and Am9 climbs, tops ringing — ears wide open for today.

New thing: run the four passes, bass in hand

The chart — play roots as you analyze, one pass per loop:

| C | E7 | Am | C7 |
| F | G  | C  | G  |

Pass 1 — key: no accidentals in the signature, starts and gravitates to C: key of C. Pass 2 — number the diatonic residents: C=I, Am=vi, F=IV, G=V. Pass 3 — flag the visitors: E7 and C7 don't live in C (E7 has G♯, C7 has B♭). Pass 4 — form and cadences: two four-bar phrases, the second ending V→(I) — a turnaround.

Why that worked

The visitors are the interesting part. E7 is the V of Am — a "secondary dominant", a chord borrowed for one bar to point at the vi like a signpost (hear its G♯ lean into Am's A). C7 is the V of F, doing the same job for the IV. Analysis converts mystery chords into arrows — and arrows tell a bassist exactly what to play: leading-tone approaches into whatever the arrow points at.

Tonicization vs modulation, in one breath

A visitor pointing somewhere for a bar (E7→Am) is tonicization — a day trip. When the song actually moves in and changes its key signature, that's modulation. Day trips need an approach note; a move needs you to re-run pass 1. The full worked method: harmonic analysis.
