Tune up

Warm-up: triads, three moods

A major, A minor, A diminished arpeggios — say the moods as you play.

New thing: the four seventh flavors

Same A root, four stacks — each one is a triad plus one more note on top. Play each arpeggio slowly and give the flavor a word of your own: dreamy? bluesy? mellow? anxious? The words you pick will stick better than anyone else's.

Why that worked

A seventh chord is 1-3-5 plus the 7th — and the four combinations of major/minor third with major/minor seventh give the four flavors: maj7 (major 3rd + major 7th — dreamy, at rest), 7 ("dominant" — major 3rd + ♭7 — bluesy, pulling somewhere), m7 (minor 3rd + ♭7 — mellow, neutral), and m7♭5 (everything lowered — anxious, wants out).

And the symbols are grammar, not code: the capital letter is the root, then modifiers read left to right — "m" makes the third minor, "7" adds the flat seventh, "maj7" adds the major one. C7 and Cmaj7 are different chords; the word "maj" belongs to the seventh, not the triad. That one sentence prevents years of confusion.

What the bassist actually plays over sevenths

Rarely the whole stack. The workhorse is still root and fifth; the ♭7 is the first color worth adding (root–5–♭7 outlines a dominant chord unmistakably); the third stays the mood-teller. Where each flavor lives inside a key — and why the V chord is always a dominant 7 — is next session's story, with the full tour in seventh chords.

Apply it: play straight from symbols

A real chart moment: four symbols, no tab until you check. Read each symbol out loud in plain words first ("C major seven… A minor seven…"), find the root, then walk the arpeggio the chart implies.

This progression — Cmaj7 · Am7 · Dm7 · G7 — is the famous "circle" turnaround; you'll meet it in a hundred songs.
