New thing: read a real-world chart

Here's a song the way a bandleader would text it to you — Nashville numbers, key of C, one cell per bar:

KEY: C          (1=C  4=F  5=G  6-=Am)

VERSE   | 1 | 6- | 4 | 5 |   (play twice)
CHORUS  | 4 | 5  | 1 | 6- |
        | 4 | 5  | 1 | 1  |
FORM: Verse ×2 → Chorus → Verse → Chorus ×2

Decode it out loud first ("one is C, six-minus is A minor…"), then play the whole form, roots only, counting bars.

Why that worked

A chart compresses a song to what a rhythm section needs: the key, the bars, the changes, the form. Numbers instead of letters (Session 17 cashing in), a minus or dash for minor, one cell per bar. The two floor plans that cover most of popular music: verse–chorus (what you just played) and AABA — two verses, a contrasting bridge, verse home — the classic 32-bar shape of the standards.

The bassist's job at the section seams: announce them. A fill, an approach, or just a firm root — the band changes rooms and you're the one opening the door.

Repeats, endings, and staying found

Real charts add repeat signs, first/second endings ("1.___ 2.___"), D.S. jumps and codas — traffic signs, all learnable in an evening. The deeper skill is chunking: memorize sections as units ("verse = 1 6- 4 5"), then just remember the order. Full chart grammar with examples: song forms & charts.

Apply it: an AABA standard

The other great floor plan, in G — eight bars per section:

KEY: G          (1=G  2-=Am  4=C  5=D  6-=Em)

A  | 1 | 6- | 2- | 5 |  (×2 — that's A A)
B  | 4 | 4  | 1  | 1 |
   | 2-| 2- | 5  | 5 |   ← the bridge "goes somewhere"
A  | 1 | 6- | 2- | 5 |

Play it root-fifth, and mark each section's first beat with a firm accent — you're announcing the rooms now.
