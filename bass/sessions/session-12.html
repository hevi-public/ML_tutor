Session 12 · Phase 3 — Theory in your hands

Keys and the circle

Move yesterday's box up a fifth and exactly one note changes. Do that twelve times and you've walked through every key in music and back home — that walk has a name, and today you play it.

Tune up

Warm-up: the G box

One octave of G major, up and down, degrees sung. It's the reference point for everything today.

New thing: move up a fifth, listen for the click

Play the G box. Now start the same shape from D (A string, fret 5) and listen hard to the 7th degree. In G major that note was C; in D major it's C♯ — one semitone, one single changed note, and everything else survives the move.

Why that worked

A key is a home note plus its major-scale family. Move home up a fifth and the recipe forces exactly one resident to sharpen (the new 7th). Move up another fifth, one more sharp. Arrange all twelve homes by fifths and you get the circle of fifths — music's subway map. Neighbors share six of seven notes; opposite sides barely speak.

The key signature — that cluster of sharps or flats at the start of a staff — is just the changed residents listed once, so the page doesn't nag you note by note.

Tap any key — majors outside, relative minors inside. Each tap shows the signature and plays the root.

And the flat direction?

Walk the circle the other way — down a fifth (or up a fourth, same thing) — and instead of gaining sharps you gain flats: F has one, B♭ two, E♭ three… Bass players live on this side of the circle more than most, because horns love flat keys. Apply it: walk the circle on the neck

Here's the beautiful part: "up a fifth" is a shape you already own — next string, two frets up (Session 9). So the circle isn't abstract: it's a physical walk your hand can do.

Say each root as you land. C–G–D–A–E one way, C–F–B♭–E♭ the other — those eight cover ninety percent of the songs you'll ever be handed.

Wrap up

Keys are moved boxes, signatures are their paperwork, and the circle is a walk your fretting hand can physically do. Theory is turning out to be geography.
