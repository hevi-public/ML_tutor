Session 29 · Phase 5 — Styles

Jazz walking: your walk goes to work

Session 21 built the note-choosing machine; today it learns to swing. Jazz walking is a conversation held at a stroll — steady quarters with a triplet undertow, and a skip when you've got something to say.

Tune up

Warm-up: the formula, cold

One chorus of Dm7 · G7 · C from Session 21, straight, roles spoken.

New thing: swing the walk

Two changes turn your walk into jazz. First, the feel: quarters lean with the ride cymbal's triplet undertow — long-ish, connected, each note leaning into the next. Second, the skip: once or twice a chorus, a beat cracks into a swung pair (the ▶ button leans them for you). That's it. That's the suit.

Why that worked

Jazz walking is a conversation: your quarters are the sentence everyone leans on; the skip is an aside — used sparingly, it says "I'm listening" to the drummer's comping. The line's grammar is still Session 21's formula; the chromatic freedom grows (jazz tolerates — loves — more approach notes per bar), and the feel is borrowed from Session 25's shuffle, smoothed from a strut to a stroll.

Steal the feel: listening list

Ray Brown with the Oscar Peterson Trio ("Night Train" — the textbook), Paul Chambers on "So What" (walking as architecture), Ron Carter on "Autumn Leaves" with Miles (space and skips). Count quarters along; notice how few skips the masters actually spend. More in jazz walking.
