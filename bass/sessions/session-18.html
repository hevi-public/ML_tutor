Session 18 · Phase 4 — Grooves & lines

Tone is in your hands

Before you ever touch an amp knob, your two hands hold five tone controls. Today you play one simple line many ways and discover you've owned a mixing desk since Session 1.

Tune up

Warm-up: I–IV–V by numbers

In G, roots and fifths, numbers spoken.

New thing: one line, three plucking spots

Play the root–fifth line on the card three times: plucking over the end of the neck (fat, round, dub-like), in the middle (your everyday sound), and right by the bridge (tight, punchy, growly). Move nothing but your plucking hand's anchor point.

Why that worked

Near the bridge the string barely swings, so plucking there emphasizes its tight, trebly partials; over the neck it swings widest and blooms warm and deep. That's built-in EQ, no knobs. The other hand-controls: how hard you pluck (harder = brighter, not just louder), note length (staccato thump vs singing legato — the biggest one nobody notices), where you fret, and which string you choose for the same pitch (A on the E string ≠ A on the open A).

Dynamics — loud and soft — are relative: a groove only sounds big because something before it was small. And an accent is one note leaning louder, never faster.

OK, and the amp?

Amp EQ is the same idea with electricity: bass knob = neck-ish warmth, treble = bridge-ish snap. Set it flat, get your hands making the sounds first, then let the amp exaggerate what your hands already do. The full tour (plus palm muting and pick tone) is in tone & dynamics.

Apply it: the dynamics ladder

Same line, four intensities — whisper, speak, project, shout — then accents: everything medium except beat 1 leaning out. If a neighbor could hear only your dynamics, they should still know where bar lines are.
