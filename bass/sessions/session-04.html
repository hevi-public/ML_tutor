New thing: hold the floor under two chords

Imagine a guitarist swaying between an E chord and an A chord, four beats each. Your job: play the chord's name-note — E under the E chord, A under the A chord — and land the change exactly with them. One long note per chord is a complete, professional bass line. Really.

Tap ▶ on the card to hear the chords ring over your two notes.

Why that worked

The note a chord is named after is its root, and playing roots on the beat is the bassist's actual job description: you're the floor the chords stand on and the pulse the band walks to — harmony's anchor and rhythm's anchor, in one instrument. That's why one whole note, placed exactly, beats a flurry of clever ones.

Notice what mattered: the moment of the change. Nail the root on beat 1 of each new chord and everything sounds intentional; smear it and even right notes sound wrong.

Will it always be the root? (a peek ahead)

No — thirds, fifths and walking lines are coming. But the pros' secret is that even the fanciest line usually touches the root exactly where the ear needs the floor: beat 1, chord change. Learn the plain version so deeply that the fancy versions stay honest. The full argument lives in the bassist's job.

Apply it: the three-chord song

Now add D. E for a bar, A for a bar, D for a bar, back to A — in quarter notes it's exactly the four-string tour from Session 1. You've been playing a song all along; now you know why it works.

Push it: whole notes at first, then quarters, then try two plucks per beat with strict alternation. Same line, three energy levels — that's called arranging.
