Session 15 · Phase 3 — Theory in your hands

Triads: chords one note at a time

A chord is three notes stacked; a bassist plays them one at a time and calls it an arpeggio. Learn three small shapes today and your root-only lines suddenly get a vocabulary.

Tune up

Warm-up: the pentatonic box

E minor pentatonic up and down, eyes closed by the third loop.

New thing: major, minor, diminished — three arpeggio shapes

All from the same A root. Major: root, third up one string one fret back, fifth up one string two frets on — the happy stack. Minor: same but the third drops a fret. Diminished: minor with the fifth squeezed down a fret too. Play each slowly and name what you hear: open, sad, clenched.

Why that worked

A triad is degrees 1-3-5 of a scale sounded together; play them in sequence and it's an arpeggio. The third sets the mood (major vs minor — you knew that already), the fifth adds stability, and the root names the whole thing. Those three notes are the chord tones — the notes that can't be wrong while their chord is sounding, which makes them a bassist's ammunition, ranked: root strongest, fifth safest, third most colorful.

Why diminished sounds "clenched"

Stack two minor thirds and the outer interval is a tritone — six semitones, the most restless sound in the system. Diminished triads live on the major scale's 7th degree, wanting resolution. You'll rarely park on one, but you'll pass through them — details in triads.

Apply it: upgrade the three-chord song

Session 4's E–A–D song, three energy levels: roots only (you own this), then root–fifth (the country/rock workhorse), then the full root–third–fifth climb. Feel your line get richer while the job stays the same.

Root–fifth secret: the fifth also lives below the root — same fret, one string down. Low fifths sound huge.
