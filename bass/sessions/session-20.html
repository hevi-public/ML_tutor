Session 20 · Phase 4 — Grooves & lines

Approach notes

The difference between a bassist who plays the changes and one who announces them is usually one note — placed just before the change, one fret away from it. Today you learn the announcement.

Tune up

Warm-up: pocket check

One minute of root eighths on A at 84, placement chosen.

New thing: hear the announcement

Two versions of the same two-chord vamp. Version A: plain roots. Version B: identical, except the last beat before each change plays a note one fret below the next root. Listen to how version B makes the change sound planned.

Why that worked

That leaning note is an approach note: tension placed on a weak beat, resolved on the strong one. One fret below (or above) the target is a chromatic approach — it works from anywhere to anywhere, no theory required, because the half-step pull you've been meeting since Session 11 does all the work. The other two flavors: scale approaches (walk down the key's ladder into the target) and dominant approaches (jump to the fifth above, then land).

The golden rule: the approach lives on the weak last beat; the target owns beat 1. Announce, then arrive — never the reverse.

The enclosure — approach from both sides

Jazz's favorite garnish: surround the target — one fret above, one below, then land (or scale-above, chromatic-below). Two announcements make the arrival feel inevitable. Double-chromatic runs (two frets out, sliding in) live in approach notes with played examples of every flavor.

Apply it: announce every change

The I–V–vi–IV in G, with an approach into every chord — mix from-below and from-above until each seam has a favorite. This is the exercise that upgrades every song you already know.

Band practice: I–V–vi–IV in G, 96 bpm — the machine plays plain roots; you play the version with announcements. Hear the difference you're adding.

Quick check

Wrap up

Tension on the weak beat, arrival on the strong one — you now own the move that separates "playing along" from "leading the band somewhere".
