Session 26 · Phase 5 — Styles

Funk: the sixteenth grid

Funk cuts each beat into four and treats every slot as sacred — some notes speak, some just click, and the silences are load-bearing. Today you learn the grid, the ghosts, and The One.

Tune up

Warm-up: count the grid

One note, sixteenths, counted out loud: "1-e-&-a 2-e-&-a…" — the mouth learns the grid before the hands need it.

New thing: ghost notes

Lay your fretting hand flat across the strings — not pressing, just touching — and pluck. That dead click is a ghost note: rhythm without pitch, the percussion hiding inside your bass. Now the classic funk bar: real notes on the strong slots, ghosts (the × heads) filling the gaps.

Why that worked

Funk's engine is the sixteenth grid: sixteen slots a bar, each one either a note, a ghost, or a deliberate silence — and the groove is the pattern of those choices, repeated until it hypnotizes. Ghosts keep your hands moving through the gaps (so the time never floats) while giving the drums something to interlock with. And everything — everything — resolves to The One: beat 1 hit hard, the anchor James Brown built an empire on.

Steal the feel: listening list

Bootsy Collins on James Brown's "Sex Machine" (The One, incarnate), Rocco Prestia with Tower of Power "What Is Hip?" (the sixteenth grid as weather), Flea "Give It Away" (ghosts with distortion). Count "1-e-&-a" along until you hear which slots each bassist chose. More in funk.

Apply it: the vamp with the band

Funk lives on one chord for minutes at a time — the interest is rhythmic. Loop the bar with the machine, then start moving ghosts to different slots and hear the groove change species.

Band: E7 vamp, funk 16ths, 92.

Quick check

Wrap up

Grid, ghosts, The One — your right hand is now a drummer too. When the click in the gaps starts happening without thought, you've joined a rhythm section that never runs out of work.
