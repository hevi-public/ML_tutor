New thing: the thumb and the snap

Slap: bounce the side of your thumb's knuckle off the E string right at the neck's end — strike and rebound like a drumstick, never press. Pop: hook your index fingertip under the D or G string, pull it away and let it snap back against the frets. Thumb = kick drum, pop = snare — you're a rhythm section in one hand.

Why that worked

Slap works because the string striking the frets adds a percussive click to the note — pitch and drum in one gesture (Larry Graham invented it to replace a missing drummer; the style still carries the whole beat). Harmonics work by touching — not pressing — the string at a division point (frets 12, 7, 5) so it vibrates in halves, thirds, quarters: glassy bell tones from thin air. And tapping is your Session 10 hammer-on with the plucking hand joining in — two hands, two voices.

The fifth string, and other roads

Five-string basses add a low B — five more notes of floor, same fourths logic, everything you learned transfers untouched. Fretless, picks, effects: all roads, none mandatory. The full techniques tour (with slap and harmonics listening lists) is in advanced techniques.
