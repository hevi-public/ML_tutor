Session 27 · Phase 5 — Styles

Soul & Motown: singable lines

In Detroit in the sixties, a bassist named James Jamerson started playing lines people could hum — and the bass stopped being furniture. Today you build a singable line: chord tones for bones, syncopation for heartbeat.

Tune up

Warm-up: ghosts, briefly

Half a minute of yesterday's ghost bar — Motown syncopation borrows funk's grid, gentler.

New thing: a line you could hum

Over C–Am–F–G (the doo-wop from Session 17). The moves that make it Motown: a pushed note (landing on the "&" before the beat), an octave leap for lift, and a scale walk connecting chords. Learn it exactly, then sing it away from the bass — the real test of the style.

Why that worked

Soul bass is melody wearing a rhythm section's uniform: every note still a chord tone or an approach (nothing you don't know), but phrased like a singer — syncopation (weight on the off-beats) in service of the song, never against it. The pushed "&" creates forward yearning; the octave leap is the line taking a breath; and everything supports the vocal, because at Motown the song was the boss.

Steal the feel: listening list

James Jamerson on "What's Going On" (the Sistine Chapel of bass lines), "My Girl" (restraint as genius), Duck Dunn on "Sittin' On the Dock of the Bay" (Memphis's answer). Hum each bass line from memory — if you can, the bassist did their job. More in soul & Motown.

Apply it: serve the song

Run the line with the machine, then the discipline drill: one chorus plain roots, one chorus the full line — and feel how the fancy version only works because the plain one lives inside it.

Band: C–Am–F–G, Motown pulse, 96.

Quick check

Wrap up

Chord tones, approaches, a push and a leap — you knew every ingredient; Motown taught you the phrasing. If you caught yourself humming the line while making coffee, mark this session twice.
