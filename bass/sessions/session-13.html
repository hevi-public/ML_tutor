New thing: the A minor box

Start on A (E string, fret 5, index this time) and follow the tab. Every note in this scale also lives in C major — yet it doesn't sound like sunshine at all, because home has moved to A.

Why that worked

That was the natural minor scale — recipe W-H-W-W-H-W-W (frets +2 +1 +2 +2 +1 +2 +2). A minor and C major hold identical notes; each is the other's relative. What changed is gravity: phrases now rest on A, and the little 3rd degree — C, a minor third above home, your "sad" shape from Session 9 — colors everything.

Different question, different twin: keep the same home and just swap the scale's mood — A major versus A minor — and that pair is called parallel. Relative = same notes, new home. Parallel = same home, new notes.

Three minors in one breath

Natural minor is the raw material. Harmonic minor raises the 7th one fret so it leans home hard (instant Bond-villain flavor — you'll taste it in the exercise). Melodic minor smooths the resulting gap for rising melodies. Bassists mostly live in natural minor; knowing why the other two exist is enough for now — the full story is in minor scales.

Apply it: flip a song's mood

The fastest ear-training on bass: change one note — the third — and flip a line from major to minor. Then taste harmonic minor's raised 7th. And yes: E–G–A, your day-one riff? That's the spine of E minor. You've been a minor-key player since Session 1.

Wrap up

Two homes, two twins (relative and parallel), and a one-fret mood switch under your fingers. Your ear now files every song it hears into one of two boxes — that's a superpower disguised as sadness.
