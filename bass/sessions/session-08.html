New thing: the three-chord song, waltzing

Same E–A–D–A song as Session 4 — but now each bar holds three beats. Count "1 2 3" with a fat accent on the 1, and feel how the music starts to sway instead of march.

Why that worked

The fraction at the start of the staff is the time signature: the top number says how many beats fill a bar, the bottom says which note value counts as one beat. 4/4 — four quarters — is so standard it's literally called common time. 3/4 is the waltz: three quarters, heavy downbeat, sway.

What changed for you as a bassist? Only the count. The job — root on the 1, pulse locked — survives every meter.

6/8 vs 3/4 — the classic trap

Both contain six eighth notes, so on paper they look like twins. The difference is grouping: 3/4 is three steady quarter beats (ONE-two-three), 6/8 is two big lopes of three eighths each (ONE-and-a-TWO-and-a). Count a 6/8 song in 3/4 and you'll fight the drummer all night. The details — plus what the bassist does in each — are in time signatures.
