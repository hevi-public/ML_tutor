Session 6 · Phase 2 — Fretboard & rhythm

The fretboard is a machine

Ninety-six places to put a finger looks like chaos. It's actually a machine with three rules — and once your hands know them, "where is C?" stops being a memory question and becomes a one-second calculation.

Tune up

Warm-up: the box riff

Session 3's E–G–A riff, one relaxed pass — those two dots at frets 3 and 5 are about to grow into a whole navigation system.

New thing: the octave jump

Play open E. Now go two strings up (to the D string) and two frets up (fret 2) — play that. Same note, higher floor: an octave. That two-up-two-over jump keeps its shape everywhere on the neck. Try it from G (fret 3) and A (fret 5).

Why that worked

The machine's three rules: Rule 1 — each string is tuned five frets (a fourth) above its neighbor, which is why fret 5 always equals the next open string. Rule 2 — twelve frets up, the alphabet wraps: fret 12 is the same letter as the open string, one octave higher (that's the double dot). Rule 3 — because rules 1 and 2 never change, every shape you learn works from any starting note. The octave jump is just rule 1 twice (10 frets of pitch) plus 2 frets.

Tap around the map — every E, A and D under fret 12 is marked:

Notice the landmarks: dots at 3–5–7–9, double at 12. Low-string names at those dots (G–A–B on E; C–D–E on A) unlock almost every root you'll need for months.

One sound, two names?

The note between F and G answers to F♯ ("F sharp — F raised a fret") and G♭ ("G flat — G lowered a fret"). Same fret, same sound, two spellings; which name is "right" depends on the key you're in — that story starts in Session 12. Until then: sharps going up, flats coming down is a fine habit. Apply it: the note hunt

Use the rules, not memory: fret 5 = next open string, twelve = wrap, octave shape = two-and-two. Say each note's name as you land on it.

Then make it a sport: the fretboard trainer deals you sixty seconds of "find the note" — a daily round for the next two weeks and the neck is yours.

Wrap up

Three rules, a set of landmarks, and an octave jump your hand already trusts. The neck is now a map, not a maze.
