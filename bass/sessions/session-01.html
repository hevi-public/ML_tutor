Session 1 · Phase 1 — First sounds

Day one: make it rumble

Bass in hand? Good. In the next twenty minutes you'll make your first sounds, tune them by ear, and play a real four-string bass line with a steady pulse. Reading about music starts later — today you play.

Get ready

One minute, tops — then you're playing.

  • Sit or stand — your call. If you have a strap, set it so the bass hangs at the same height either way; future-you will be glad the two feel identical.
  • Body against you, neck angled slightly up. Sitting: the bass's waist rests on your right thigh.
  • Right forearm rests on the top edge of the body, hand hanging relaxed over the strings.
  • Left hand cradles the neck — thumb on the back, fingers curled loosely over the strings. It does nothing else today.
  • No amp? No problem. Unplugged is plenty loud for everything in this session.

Playing left-handed? Mirror everything, today and always.

Make your first sound

Rest your right thumb on the pickup — the plastic-looking bar sitting under the strings. Now put the side of your index fingertip against the thickest string, and pull across it, toward the floor-side string — not up and away. Let it ring.

That low rumble is the open E — the lowest note your bass has, and the foundation half the songs you know are standing on.

Tune up

You've made four sounds — now make them the right four sounds. Tap a pad, play the matching string, and turn that string's peg a little at a time until the two sounds melt into one. Tighter = higher, looser = lower.

Can't tell higher from lower yet? Tune your string clearly too low, then come up slowly until the wobble dies. Every bassist started there.

Your first bass line

Four strings, one pattern, still no left hand. Count "1 2 3 4" out loud — one pluck per count, and change strings each time you say "1".

Why that worked

You just played the four open strings by name: E, A, D, G, thickest to thinnest. Every note this instrument can make starts from one of those four — which is why saying the names out loud on day one is not a beginner exercise; it's the map.

Tuning worked because a tighter string vibrates faster, and faster means higher pitch: you matched each string to its reference until the wobble between the two sounds vanished. And that loop you played — home on E, away to A and D, home again — is already a riff: a short pattern that repeats with intention. Congratulations, that's the actual job.

Why E–A–D–G, and not four random notes

Each string is tuned the same distance above its neighbor — five frets' worth, an interval called a fourth. Same gap everywhere means any pattern you learn keeps its shape when you move it across strings. You'll feel this superpower the moment your left hand joins in, two sessions from now — the full story lives in strings & fret math.
