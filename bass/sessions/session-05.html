Session 5 · Phase 1 — First sounds

Count it out loud

You've been feeling wholes, quarters and doubled-up notes for four sessions. Today they get their names, and you get the counting language — spoken out loud, yes, actually out loud — that lets you play a rhythm you've never heard.

Tune up

Warm-up: the three-chord song

Session 4's E–A–D–A, one pass in quarters. Say the chord names on the changes.

New thing: the value ladder

One note — open A — four rhythms. Each bar holds the same four beats; the notes just slice them differently. Count out loud exactly as written under the notes: numbers on the beats, "&" (say "and") between them.

Why that worked

Every bar in 4/4 holds four beats, and note values just say how many of them a note keeps: a whole note keeps all four, a half keeps two, a quarter keeps one, and an eighth splits the beat in two — the doubled-up feel you found in Session 2, now with a name.

Counting aloud works because your mouth is a better metronome than your mind: thinking drifts, speech doesn't. Every serious player counted aloud for years — the ones who skip it are the ones who rush.

Dots and ties, in one breath

A dot after a note stretches it by half again — a dotted half holds three beats. A tie (a curved line joining two notes) glues their lengths into one sound, which is how written music holds a note across a barline. You'll read both constantly; the full tour with played examples is in rhythm & counting.

Apply it: holes and holds

Real bass lines are made of notes and silences. A rest is a silence with a length — you count it, you just don't play it. Mute the string with a flat left hand the moment the rest starts.

Bar 3 rests on beat 1 — the hardest beat to not play. Count louder there. (Reggae builds a whole style on that hole; you'll meet it in Session 28.)

Wrap up

Phase 1 complete. You tune by ear, your right hand has an engine, your left hand has landmarks, you know the job, and you speak the counting language. That's a real foundation.
