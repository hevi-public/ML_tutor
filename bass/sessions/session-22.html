Session 22 · Phase 4 — Grooves & lines

The 12-bar blues

If music has one shared campfire, it's twelve bars of blues. Learn the map today and you can walk into any jam on the planet, ask "blues in A?", and belong.

Tune up

Warm-up: walk the handshake

One chorus of Dm7 · G7 · C from memory — roles said aloud.

New thing: the map, roots only

Twelve bars, three chords, in A: four bars of I (A7), two of IV (D7), two of I, then the turn: V (E7), IV, I, V. Play roots through the whole form and count the bars out loud — knowing where you are is the entire skill.

Why that worked

The 12-bar blues is a form — a repeating floor plan everyone memorizes so nobody needs a chart. Its grammar in numbers: I-I-I-I · IV-IV-I-I · V-IV-I-V. Every chord is a dominant 7th (that bluesy lean from Session 16 — in blues, tension never fully resolves, and that's the sound). The final V is the turnaround: it spins the wheel back to bar 1.

The quick change

Common variant: bar 2 jumps early to IV, back to I for bars 3-4 — the "quick change". At a jam someone just says "quick change" and everyone adjusts one bar. Turnaround vocabulary (that last V dressed up with approaches) and more variants: the 12-bar blues.

Apply it: the line every bassist owns

The boogie line — root, 3, 5, 6 on each chord — older than electricity and still on the radio. Learn it on A7, move the same shape to D7 and E7 (it's movable, of course), then play the whole form.

The band's waiting: 12-bar in A, shuffle, 96 — play the boogie line against it and count the bars. Lose your place? Keep grooving on the root till bar 1 comes round; that's a real-jam skill too.

Quick check

Wrap up

Twelve bars, three chords, one movable line — and the map lives in your count now, not on paper. You're jam-ready in any key that starts with "blues in…".
