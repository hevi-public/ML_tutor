Session 17 · Phase 4 — Grooves & lines

Numbers for progressions

"It's a one-four-five in G" — and every musician in the room knows the whole song. Today you learn the number language, play the three most famous progressions in music, and feel why the V chord always wants to go home.

Tune up

Warm-up: the chart drill

Cmaj7 · Am7 · Dm7 · G7 roots, symbols spoken aloud.

New thing: the one, the four, the five

Number the G major scale's notes 1-7. Build a chord on each and number those too. Now play the progression I–IV–V–I in G: roots G, C, D, G. Congratulations — you just played the skeleton of a few thousand songs.

Why that worked

Every major key grows the same seven-chord family from its scale — same qualities in the same slots, every key. So musicians name chords by slot number: Roman numerals in writing (uppercase major: I, IV, V; lowercase minor: ii, iii, vi; the odd one out vii°), or spoken Nashville numbers ("one, four, five"). Learn a song as numbers and you know it in all twelve keys at once.

And the pull you feel from V back to I? The V chord contains the key's 7th degree — the leaner — aching that last half step home. That ache is called a cadence, and it's the engine every chorus runs on.

The family, spelled out in G

I=G · ii=Am · iii=Bm · IV=C · V=D · vi=Em · vii°=F♯dim. Same pattern in every major key — swap the roots, keep the qualities. Minor keys have their own family (i, iv, v get moody). The full table, plus why this buys you instant jam-session literacy, is in diatonic harmony.

Apply it: the famous three, by numbers

Three progressions cover most of the radio: I–V–vi–IV (the pop machine), I–vi–IV–V (the doo-wop), ii–V–I (the jazz handshake). Play each in G from the numbers, then prove the point: play one of them in C without any new learning.

Then with the band: I–V–vi–IV in G · doo-wop · ii–V–I.

You speak numbers now — the language that makes every key the same key and every jam session readable. The theory phase paid off; from here it's craft.
