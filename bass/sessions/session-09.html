Interval shapes and their sounds

Five small hand-shapes, one bonus reach — that's the entire melodic alphabet of the bass. Learn each shape's sound today and you'll never again wonder what a line will sound like before you play it.

New thing: the shape tour

Home base: A at fret 5 of the E string. Every pair in this tour starts there and reaches for one shape. Play each pair slowly, twice, and listen for its personality — solid, bright, hollow, sweet, sad, bluesy.

Why that worked

The distance between two notes is an interval, measured in semitones — and on bass, every interval is a shape that never changes. Next string same fret: a fourth (5 semitones, open and sturdy). Next string two frets up: a fifth (7 — the power-chord sound). Two strings and two frets: the octave (12 — "same but higher"). Next string one fret back: a major third (4, sweet); two back: a minor third (3, sad). Bonus reach — two strings straight up: a minor seventh (10, the funky one).

Because the neck's rules never change, the shape is the sound. Grab any root, make the fifth shape, and you already know what you'll hear.

All six targets from the A root. Tap them; say the interval, then the note name.

Why "major", "minor", "perfect"?

Thirds, sixths and sevenths come in two sizes — the bigger is "major", the smaller "minor". Fourths, fifths and octaves come in one size so pure the old theorists called them "perfect". You don't need the full taxonomy yet; your ear is collecting the sounds, which is the part that can't be crammed later. Apply it: hum first, then play

The shapes only pay off when the sound arrives before the finger. Reverse the flow: play the root, hum where you think the interval lands, then play the second note and grade yourself.

Then let the machine quiz you: the ear trainer plays intervals cold and keeps score. Two minutes a day beats twenty on Sunday.
