Session 25 · Phase 5 — Styles

Blues shuffle: the triplet lean

Take yesterday's straight eighths and let each pair lean — long-short, long-short, like a limp with swagger. That lean is the shuffle, it's older than the electric bass, and your 12-bar map is about to groove.

Tune up

Warm-up: straight vs leaned

Listen first: the same bar played straight, then shuffled. Then play both on open A — say "DOO-da DOO-da" for the leaned one.

Written identically — the lean is a performance instruction, exactly how real charts do it ("shuffle feel").

New thing: the shuffle line over the blues

The R-3-5-6 boogie from Session 22, now in leaning eighths — each beat's pair long-short. Play the first four bars of the A blues with it; the ▶ button demonstrates the lean.

Why that worked

The shuffle divides each beat into a hidden triplet and gives the note-pair the first two thirds and the last third — long-short. That's swing's blood relative: written as plain eighths, played with the lean, because notating triplets everywhere would bury the page. Your job is a consistent lean — every pair identical — and note length that struts rather than stumbles.

Steal the feel: listening list

Stevie Ray Vaughan "Pride and Joy" (Tommy Shannon's shuffle masterclass), T-Bone Walker "Stormy Monday" (slow shuffle dignity), ZZ Top "La Grange" (the shuffle goes to Texas). Count the lean out loud with the records. More in blues shuffle.
