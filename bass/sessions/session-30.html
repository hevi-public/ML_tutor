Modes in your hands

Modes have launched a thousand confused forum threads. Skip the confusion: a mode is one color note away from a scale you already own, heard over a drone. Today you play the two that earn rent — Dorian and Mixolydian.

Tune up

Tune — and leave the E pad droning when you're done; it's your canvas for the next step.

Warm-up: E minor, for reference

One octave of plain E natural minor over the drone — this is the "before" picture.

New thing: Dorian — minor with a bright window

Drone still humming? Play the groove: it's E minor with one changed note — C becomes C♯, the bright 6th. That single note turns brooding minor into the cool, lit-from-inside minor of funk and Santana. Hang on the C♯ and feel it glow against the drone.

Why that worked

A mode is the major scale started from a different degree — same notes, different home, and the half steps land in new places (that's the flavor). But the useful way to think is by color note: Dorian = natural minor with a raised 6. Mixolydian = major with a flat 7 — the scale that fits every dominant-7 vamp you've played since the blues. The rest of the seven have names (Phrygian's dark ♭2, Lydian's floaty ♯4, Locrian's chaos) worth recognizing more than living in.

E Dorian, whole neck. The bright spots are the C♯s — the one note doing all the mood lighting.

When do modes actually matter?

On static vamps — one chord grooving for a long time — the mode names the flavor you're painting with. In songs with moving changes, key-thinking (Session 12) covers you and modal vocabulary is mostly commentary. The full working-bassist's mode table lives in modes.

Apply it: Mixolydian over a dominant vamp

Switch the drone to A. Mixolydian is the major box with the 7th relaxed a fret — instantly familiar: it's the sound of every A7 groove you've played. Run the groove, then improvise a chorus inside the scale, leaning on the ♭7 (G) as your color.

Prefer a full band to a drone? A7 vamp, funk 16ths — Mixolydian's natural habitat.
