Session 7 · Phase 2 — Fretboard & rhythm

Read it: tab and staff

Bass has two written languages: tab's honest little numbers and the staff's floating dots. Today you play the same riff from both — and learn which language tells you what.

Tune up

Warm-up: octave jumps

Two rounds of Session 6's E and G jumps — shape as one hand-frame, names out loud.

New thing: play from the numbers

Look at the four-line grid under the notation — that's tab. Four lines = your four strings, thickest E on the bottom. A number is a fret on that string; 0 means open. Read only the numbers for now and let your ear supply the rhythm from the ▶ button.

Why that worked

The two systems split the truth between them. Tab tells your hands where — string and fret, no ambiguity, which matters on bass because the same note lives in several places. The staff tells your ears what — pitch and rhythm. Bass reads the bass clef (the fancy "9" whose two dots hug the F line): lines G–B–D–F–A bottom to top, spaces A–C–E–G.

Your open strings on the staff: E hangs below on a ledger line, A sits under the bottom line, D takes the middle line, G the fourth line. Learn those four addresses and you can triangulate everything else.

The internet-tab warning

Most tab sites skip rhythm entirely — numbers with no lengths. Fine when you already know how the song goes; useless when you don't. That's why real charts pair tab with notation (like this site does), and why the staff is worth befriending even if tab stays your first language. Full comparison in tab ↔ notation.

Apply it: now read the dots

A new phrase. This time work top-down: read the staff first — name each note from its line or space, find it on the neck yourself — and only then check your answer against the tab below it.

Cover the tab line with a finger on the first pass. Honesty now = fluency later.
