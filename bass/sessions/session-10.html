One finger per fret

So far your left hand has been improvising its fingering. Today it gets a system — four fingers, four frets, one job each — plus the two little moves (hammer and pull) that let notes sing without a pluck.

New thing: the spider

Park your hand so your index hovers over fret 5 — then middle owns 6, ring owns 7, pinky owns 8. That's the whole system: one finger per fret, nobody moonlights. Crawl it slowly across all four strings.

Why that worked

A four-fret window with a finger assigned to each fret is a position — named after the index finger's fret, so you just played "5th position". Inside a position, every note is one finger-drop away: no searching, no stretching, no wasted motion. Most bass lines live inside one position for bars at a time; when the music leaves the window, you don't reach — you shift, sliding the whole frame as one unit (shifting).

The quiet half of technique: muting

Great bassists are defined less by the notes they start than the ones they stop. Fingers that aren't fretting rest lightly on the strings; the right thumb quiets the E string when you play higher — both hands share the silence work. It develops naturally if you listen for ring-over; the full checklist is in the left hand.

Apply it: hammer-ons and pull-offs

Two notes, one pluck: pluck A (index, fret 5) and hammer the ring finger down hard onto fret 7 — the B sounds by impact alone. Reverse it: with both fingers down, pluck, then pull the ring finger off sideways with a tiny flick — it plucks the string for you on the way out.
