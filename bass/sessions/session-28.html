Session 28 · Phase 5 — Styles

Reggae: the space is the note

Every style so far worshipped beat 1. Reggae's one-drop deletes it — and the hole where the downbeat should be becomes the deepest groove in music. Today you learn to play silence on purpose.

Tune up

Warm-up: the rest on 1

Remember Session 5's hardest bar — rest ON beat 1? That was reggae training in disguise. One pass.

New thing: the one drop

The pattern: nothing on 1, a fat note on 2… let it breathe… then movement into the next bar. Count "ONE" out loud extra firmly because you're not playing it — the silence only grooves if everyone knows exactly where it is.

Why that worked

In the one drop, drums and bass leave beat 1 empty together (the kick "drops" to beat 3 with the snare's rim). The missing downbeat creates suspension — your ear leans into the hole every bar, and that lean is the sway. The bass plays fat, round, and behind the beat (Session 19's vocabulary, chosen deliberately): neck-side plucking, long notes, laid-back placement. Space isn't absence here; it's the loudest thing in the bar.

Steal the feel: listening list

Aston "Family Man" Barrett on Bob Marley's "Natural Mystic" (the one-drop constitution), "Stir It Up" (melody in molasses), Robbie Shakespeare on "Uptown Top Ranking" (minimalism as law). Notice how LOUD the empty beat 1 feels. More in reggae.

Apply it: sway with the band

The machine plays a proper one drop — skank chops on 2 and 4, bass holes on 1. Lock into it, then try the discipline test: can you keep the hole empty for three whole minutes without your rock reflexes filling it?

Band: A vamp, one drop, 72 — slow is correct; reggae walks, never runs.

Quick check

Wrap up

You can now not play the most important beat in the bar and make it groove harder for the absence. That's the deepest lesson in the styles tour: space is a note, and you choose it.
