How to read an ML paper

Papers are written backwards, reviewed under deadline, and padded for prestige — and they're still where the field actually lives. Here's how practitioners extract the signal.

Before you start Everything before this page IS the preparation: you can read the notation, you know the vocabulary, and you've trained the models papers take as given.

The shape of the beast

Nearly every ML paper is the same eight parts: title/abstract (the sales pitch), introduction (the sales pitch, longer), related work (politics and genealogy), method (the actual idea, in armor-plated notation), experiments (the evidence), ablations (which parts of the idea actually matter — often the most honest section), limitations (increasingly real, historically decorative), appendix (where the reproducibility details hide).

The single most useful fact about this genre: the idea is usually one sentence, and the paper is thirty pages of armor around it. Attention's paper is "let every position query every other directly." Diffusion's is "learn to undo noise, then start from pure noise." Your job as a reader is to find that sentence, not to absorb the armor.

Three passes, not one

  1. Pass 1 — five minutes, no honor at stake. Title, abstract, all figures and their captions, conclusion. ML papers put their soul in Figure 1 far more often than in Section 3. Exit question: what's the one-sentence idea, and do I care?
  2. Pass 2 — thirty minutes, pencil out. Read for structure, skipping proofs and derivations. For every equation, do what this site trained you to do: find the verb, name each symbol aloud, translate to a plain sentence — most fearsome equations are "weighted average" or "our loss has two terms" in armor. Look hard at the experiments' baselines: what is this beating, and is that comparison fair (same data? same compute? tuned equally hard)?
  3. Pass 3 — hours, only for the few that earn it. Re-derive, or better: re-implement small. You've already done this once — the autodiff page was a pass-3 of the backprop literature. One paper truly rebuilt beats fifty skimmed, permanently.

The skeptic's checklist

  • "SOTA" (state-of-the-art) by how much, at what cost? +0.2% from doubled compute is a shrug wearing a tuxedo. Check the compute column — it's the field's most quietly-omitted number.
  • Where did the test set come from — and is it leaking? Unit 2's honesty rules at field scale: benchmark questions have a way of seeping into training data, especially for LLMs trained on the whole internet (which now contains the benchmarks).
  • Are the ablations present? A paper claiming five innovations but ablating none is telling you it doesn't know which parts work either. Often one component carries everything.
  • Cherry-picked outputs? Generative-model papers show their best samples, never a random draw. Look for the random-sample grid or note its absence loudly.
  • Wrong-by-omission baselines: the strongest known simple method (a tuned random forest; plain fine-tuning; last year's model at equal compute) has a strange way of not appearing in tables it would win.

An annotated first reading list

Ordered to match this site's path — each is the canonical source for a page you've already mastered, which makes them the gentlest possible entry into the literature:

  • "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., 2017) — the transformer. After the attention page, Figure 1 will read like an old friend. Skip Section 5's training minutiae on pass 1.
  • "Deep Residual Learning" (He et al., 2015) — one-sentence idea: "let layers learn corrections to identity instead of whole transformations." The cleanest example of a tiny idea moving a whole field.
  • "Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models" (Ho et al., 2020) — the diffusion page's source. Heavy math armor; you already know the sentence inside it.
  • "Language Models are Few-Shot Learners" (Brown et al., 2020, the GPT-3 paper) — where prompting-instead-of-fine-tuning entered the world. Read the ablations on model scale.
  • "Random Forests" (Breiman, 2001) — older, wonderfully readable, and the argument for WHY averaging decorrelated trees works is the clearest statistics writing in the field's canon.

Sequence for building the habit: one paper per week, pass 1 always, pass 2 if it survives, pass 3 quarterly. A year of that and you're current; the literature only looks infinite from outside.
