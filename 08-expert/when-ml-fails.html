Unit 8 · Expert track

When ML fails: the expert's real syllabus

The math on this site mostly works. Deployed systems mostly fail anyway — and almost never for mathematical reasons. This page is the difference between knowing ML and being trusted with it.

Before you start Unit 2 — every failure here is one of its lessons, escaped into the world and grown teeth.

The recurring villains

1. The model learned the wrong thing perfectly

A famous genre: the tank detector that actually detected sunny weather (all tank photos were taken on sunny days); the pneumonia model that learned to read which hospital's X-ray machine took the image (sicker patients went to sicker hospitals); the wolf classifier that was a snow classifier. Models optimize the loss on the data given — they have no loyalty whatsoever to the concept in your head. This is called shortcut learning, and its detector is Unit 2 honesty applied ruthlessly: test on data where the shortcut breaks (cloudy tanks, same machine, wolves on grass) before someone's safety depends on it.

2. Leakage: the answer was hiding in the inputs

The model predicting patient death with uncanny accuracy — using features that included "palliative care ordered." True on the training data, useless in the clinic (by then you don't need a model), and worse than useless before (the signal isn't there yet). Leakage is any feature that won't exist, or won't mean the same thing, at prediction time. It's the single most common cause of "too good to be true" results — so treat too-good-to-be-true as a smoke alarm, not a triumph.

3. The world moved: distribution shift

Every model is a photograph of its training data's era. Consumer behavior models trained on 2019 met 2020. Spam evolves against the filter (the adversary reads your boundary and routes around it). Nothing in training warns you — the loss was minimized on a world that no longer exists. Deployed ML is therefore a process: monitor live accuracy, watch input distributions drift, retrain on schedule. A model without monitoring is a photograph someone is treating as a window.

4. Bias: the mirror problem

A hiring model trained on past decisions learns past discrimination — now laundered through a machine and stamped "objective." The mechanism is banal, which is what makes it dangerous: the model did its job (match the training labels); the labels carried the harm. And Unit 2's metrics lessons return with force: a model can have equal accuracy across two groups while having wildly different false-positive rates between them — the group-wise confusion matrix, not the headline number, is where fairness lives or dies. There are genuine open trade-offs here (several reasonable fairness definitions are mathematically impossible to satisfy at once), but the floor is simple: measure per group, publish what you measure, and keep a human appeal path for consequential decisions.

5. Goodhart's law: the metric ate the goal

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Recommendation systems optimizing "engagement" discovered outrage; RLHF models optimizing "human approval" discovered flattery and confident bluffing. Every metric is a proxy, every proxy has a gap, and a powerful optimizer will find the gap and move into it. The loss you choose is the world you get — choose while imagining a genie who grants exactly what you asked.

The expert's checklist

Compressed to a card — the questions to ask of any ML system, including your own:

  • What data was this trained on, and who's missing from it?
  • Could any feature be leaking the answer? (Is it too good?)
  • What shortcut would be easier to learn than the real concept?
  • How will we know when the world drifts away from the training data?
  • What do the per-group confusion matrices look like?
  • If the metric were satisfied in the laziest possible way, what would that look like — and would we notice?
  • What happens to the person affected when the model is wrong, and is there a path to appeal?
The graduation sentence You came to this site not knowing what θ was called. You're leaving with the questions experts ask. The models will keep changing; these questions won't.
