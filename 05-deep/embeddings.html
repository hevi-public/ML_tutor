Embeddings: meaning as a place on a map

Networks eat numbers, not words. The fix turned out to be one of the most beautiful ideas in the field: give every word a location, and let "similar meaning" become "nearby".

Before you start Vectors — an embedding IS a vector; this page is that idea finally paying off in full.

The idea

How do you feed the word "cat" to a neural network? Numbering the dictionary (cat = 5,231) is poison: it implies cat is somehow "close to" word 5,230 and "far from" word 90,000, which is meaningless. What we want is numbers where distance means something.

An embedding does exactly that: each word gets a list of, say, 300 numbers — its coordinates in a "meaning space." The coordinates are learned, not designed: train a network to predict a word from its surroundings across billions of sentences, and the only way it can succeed is by assigning similar coordinates to words used in similar ways. Meaning, as the residue of prediction.

What emerges is eerie: directions in the space become concepts. There's a direction that means "royalty", one that means "plural", one that means gender — so arithmetic on coordinates does analogy: king − man + woman lands near queen.

Why this matters beyond words

Embeddings are how deep learning handles anything discrete: products, songs, users, code, proteins. "Things used similarly get nearby coordinates" is the universal move, and it's the machinery behind recommendation engines ("people who liked nearby-things also liked…"), semantic search (embed the query, embed the documents, retrieve the nearest — the guts of every "chat with your PDF" product), and the input layer of every language model. When Unit 6 talks about LLMs, their very first act on your prompt is: look up each token's embedding.
